The JRB Skincare Opportunity

01 — Executive Summary

JRB skincare is a $5.9M business that punches below its potential

Skincare grew +11.5% YoY to $5.9M in net sales, but the portfolio has clear winners and losers. The Eye Cream ecosystem alone drives $2.3M (40% of skincare revenue). Meanwhile, the Multivitamin Serum is declining (-14%), the Miracle Cream is softening (-6%), and the existing SPF product is underperforming in a market where 44% of customers are asking for it. The opportunity: build a skincare line that reflects the "Modern Prestige Beauty" positioning — expert-formulated, skin-first, and unapologetically simple.

The Core Tension
JRB's makeup is built on a clear philosophy: "enhance, don't change." But the skincare line doesn't have an equivalent story. It's a collection of good individual products without a unifying skincare POV. Competitors like Rhode ("barrier repair"), Drunk Elephant ("clean-clinical"), and Glossier ("skin first, makeup second") all have a skincare thesis. JRB needs one — and it should flow directly from Bobbi's 35+ years of expertise: skin that's so healthy it barely needs makeup.

Three Strategic Imperatives

1. Define the POV
Expert Skin Prep
Position JRB skincare as what a legendary makeup artist would want your skin to look like BEFORE makeup. Not clinical. Not trend-chasing. The prep that makes "just enough" makeup possible.
2. Fix the Portfolio
Cut, Fix, Add
Kill or reformulate problem products (Multivitamin Serum rollerball, Oil Stick rancidity). Double down on winners (Eye Cream ecosystem). Fill the SPF gap. Launch the cleanser portfolio.
3. Build the Bridge
Skincare → Makeup
JRB's unique advantage: the founder IS a makeup artist. Every skincare product should be designed as the ideal canvas for JRB makeup. This is the bridge no competitor can build.
02 — The JRB Consumer

She's older, wealthier, and more loyal than the category — but the brand reads "mature"

Cross-referencing Tracksuit brand health data, 11 Typeform surveys, and Polar customer data paints a clear portrait: the core JRB consumer is 50-65+, has high household income ($100K+), values simplicity and expertise, and is deeply loyal. But the brand's perceived age skew creates a ceiling for growth.

The Paradox
JRB's core customer CAN afford premium — she has $100K+ household income and over-indexes on spending. But she doesn't PERCEIVE JRB as premium. The 38% "worth paying more for" score (6th of 7 brands) isn't a price problem — it's a positioning and experience problem.

What She Wants (Survey Synthesis)

  • Simplicity above all — "I don't want a 10-step routine" is the #1 theme across all surveys. She wants fewer, better products.
  • Skin-first results — She cares more about how her skin looks WITHOUT makeup than with it. "Glow" and "hydration" are her top desired outcomes.
  • Expert credibility — Bobbi Brown's name is the #1 reason she trusts the brand. But she wants products that feel expert, not just endorsed.
  • Clean but not crunchy — She cares about clean ingredients but doesn't want to sacrifice performance. "Works AND is clean" is the expectation.
  • SPF everywhere — 44% of Just Enough Tinted Moisturizer reviews mention wanting SPF. This is the single biggest unmet need.
  • Travel-friendly sizes — Mini Eye Cream did $522K in its first year. She buys minis for travel, purse, and trial.
  • Skincare benefits in makeup — Blushing Stick survey shows 70%+ want hydrating and anti-aging benefits in their blush/color products.

Target: Aspirational vs. Actual

The positioning deck targets 35+ women. The actual core is 50-65+. Upcoming brand partnerships aim to pull younger, but the skincare line has to serve both — which means formulations for mature skin concerns (hydration, fine lines, firmness) with packaging and messaging that doesn't alienate a 38-year-old.

The Eye Cream Ecosystem Is The Story
Eye Cream ($912K) + Rich Eye Cream ($913K) + Mini Eye Cream ($522K) = $2.35M combined, representing 35% of all skincare revenue. Rich Eye Cream grew +215% and is now the #1 skincare product. This suggests strong product-market fit in the eye category — consider it the anchor for the skincare line. The original Eye Cream is declining because Rich Eye Cream is cannibalizing it (not a bad thing — customers are trading up).

Winners: What's Working

  • Rich Eye Cream (+215%, 4.78 avg) — Best-rated product in the entire line. 94.5% positive, 95.8% would recommend. Multiple reviewers cite switching from Bobbi Brown Extra Eye Repair Cream. This is the anchor.
  • Light Moisture Cream (+2%, 4.72 avg) — 93.4% positive. Many reviewers explicitly say they switched FROM Miracle Cream because it was too heavy. This product is the Miracle Cream fix.
  • Hippie Stick (+32%, 4.53 avg) — 4,084 reviews — most-reviewed skincare product. Eczema and extreme dryness customers generate the strongest testimonials. 35-44 age group rates it highest (4.85).
  • Mini Eye Cream ($522K, new) — Validates the mini/travel strategy. 35K+ units at $15 — this is a trial funnel into the full-size eye cream.

Problems: What Needs Attention

  • Miracle Cream (-6%, 4.29 avg — lowest rated) — 12% negative rate. "Too heavy/greasy/sticky" is 44.9% of all negative reviews. Customers expect a cream moisturizer, get a dense balm. Team says "it should go."
  • Multivitamin Serum (-14%, 4.35 avg) — Rollerball is the #1 complaint: "cold metal ball is painful on forehead." 20% of negatives cite drying because rollerball dispenses too little. Team: "serum's probably gone."
  • Oil Stick (-5%, 4.46 avg) — Scent is 27.9% of all negative reviews: "smells like cooking oil." Plus rancidity issue from Inno Kickoff. Solvable with reformulation.
  • Cleansing Stick (4.34 avg) — 35-44 age group rates lowest (4.0 vs 4.62 for 54+). Oil-based format polarizes: "so greasy I could not get it off." 17.7% cite scent issues.
  • Mini Hippie Stick (-99.8%) — Essentially discontinued. Kill and redirect resources.

Strategic Takeaways

Opportunity
"EXPERT SKIN PREP" IS WHITE SPACE

No prestige brand credibly positions skincare as "what a legendary makeup artist wants your skin to look like before she starts." Charlotte Tilbury comes closest with Magic Cream but doesn't have Bobbi's 35+ years of actually working on skin. Rhode is barrier repair. Drunk Elephant is clean-clinical. JRB can own "the prep that makes minimal makeup possible."

Advantage
THE SKINCARE→MAKEUP BRIDGE

JRB is one of very few brands where the founder's expertise is in MAKEUP, not skincare. This means every skincare product can be designed and marketed as "what Bobbi wants your skin to look like before she puts on one drop of makeup." No skincare-first brand can make this claim. No celebrity brand can either.

Risk
SPF IS TABLE STAKES AND JRB HAS ZERO

ILIA's Super Serum Skin Tint SPF 40 is their #1 product. Rhode launched SPF. Every prestige brand has it. JRB has the Just Enough Tinted Moisturizer but NO SPF. With 44% of JET reviews asking for it, this isn't a "nice to have" — it's a gap that's actively losing customers to competitors.

06 — The JRB Skincare POV

Expert skin prep: the skincare that makes "just enough" makeup possible

The JRB skincare POV flows from the brand's core truth: Bobbi Brown spent 35+ years as a makeup artist. She knows what skin needs to look like BEFORE makeup goes on. JRB skincare isn't competing with dermatologists (Drunk Elephant) or celebrities (Rhode) or rituals (Tatcha). It's the professional prep — skin so healthy, so luminous, that you barely need makeup at all.

The Positioning

"The skincare a makeup artist would create — because one did."
Proposed skincare tagline

This positioning works because:

  • It's credible — Bobbi's expertise is in making skin look beautiful. 35+ years of working on real skin, real women, real light.
  • It's differentiated — No other skincare line comes from a makeup artist's perspective on what skin needs.
  • It connects to makeup — Every JRB skincare product is the setup for JRB makeup. The bridge is built into the DNA.
  • It justifies premium — "Expert-formulated" tests as the #2 premium driver in the Listen Labs survey design. Professional credibility commands price.
  • It serves both demos — A 38-year-old and a 62-year-old both want skin that looks great with minimal makeup. The desire is the same; the products adjust.

The Framework: Prep → Protect → Perfect

A 3-step skincare architecture that mirrors how a makeup artist preps a face:

Step 1: Prep
Clean + Treat

Cleanser portfolio (3-tier), Milky Toner, Multivitamin Serum (reformulated). Remove, reset, nourish. The canvas starts here.

Step 2: Protect
Moisturize + Shield

Miracle Cream (reformulated), Light Moisture Cream, Oil-Free Moisturizer, Eye Creams, NEW SPF. Hydrate, firm, protect. The foundation (literally) for makeup.

Step 3: Perfect
Glow + Bridge

Hippie Stick, face oils, hydrating mist. The "last step before makeup" products. These ARE the skincare-makeup bridge — the glow layer that makes "just enough" possible.

Why This Solves The "Worth Paying More For" Problem
The brand health data shows JRB's only weakness is premium perception (38% vs 44%). "Expert skin prep" directly addresses this: it elevates the products from "nice clean beauty" to "professional-grade preparation from a world-class makeup artist." The survey data confirms "created by a true expert (not just a celebrity name)" is a top premium driver. JRB has this — the skincare just needs to tell the story.

2 Which skincare steps do you NEVER skip? (Rank: Cleanser, Moisturizer, Serum, Eye Cream, SPF, Toner, Mask, Oil) Ranking Identifies which categories are non-negotiable for portfolio prioritization
3 Which JRB skincare products do you currently use, if any? (Multi-select all skincare SKUs) Multi-select Maps CAB overlap with skincare vs. makeup — are they skincare buyers already?
4 If JRB launched a complete skincare routine (cleanser → treatment → moisturizer → SPF), how interested would you be? (1-10) Scale Demand signal for the full "Expert Skin Prep" concept
5 What is the ONE skincare product you wish JRB made? (Open-ended) Open text Unaided product demand — what's in their heads that we haven't thought of?
6 What non-JRB skincare brands are in your current routine? (Open-ended) Open text Maps competitive overlap — where are we losing wallet share?
7 How important is SPF in your daily skincare routine? (Not important / Nice to have / Essential / Already in my moisturizer or foundation) Single select Validates SPF priority. "Already in my moisturizer" reveals format preference.
8 For a JRB SPF product, would you prefer: (A) Standalone SPF, (B) Moisturizer with SPF built in, (C) Tinted SPF/skin tint, (D) All of the above Single select Format preference for the SPF launch — critical product decision
9 What's the MOST you'd pay for a JRB moisturizer? (Under $30 / $30-$40 / $40-$50 / $50-$60 / $60+) Single select Price ceiling for the "Protect" step — are we priced right at $40?
10 When you think of "JRB skincare," what does that mean to you? (Open-ended) Open text Tests whether "expert skin prep" resonates or if the brand means something else in skincare context
11 How much does Bobbi Brown's expertise influence your trust in JRB skincare specifically? (Not at all / Somewhat / Very much / It's the main reason I'd trust JRB for skincare) Single select Quantifies Bobbi as RTB for skincare specifically (vs. makeup where it's assumed)
12 What's your #1 skin concern right now? (Hydration / Fine lines & wrinkles / Dark spots / Dullness / Acne / Redness / Firmness / Even skin tone) Single select Validates product development priorities — which concern should the serum reformulation target?
Survey Design Notes
Estimated completion time: 4-5 minutes (critical for CAB response rates).
Platform: Typeform (consistent with existing JRB surveys).
Target N: 200+ (standard for CAB panels).
Cross-tab by: Age (35-49 vs 50+), current JRB skincare buyer (Q3), and skin concern (Q12).
Key analysis: Q4 (interest) × Q9 (price ceiling) = willingness-to-pay matrix. Q5 (wish list) = unaided innovation pipeline. Q10 (brand meaning) = positioning validation.
10 — Open Questions

What still needs validation

This strategy is built on strong data, but several decisions need consumer validation through the Listen Labs survey (3-panel, 38 questions) and the CAB Typeform before finalizing.

Needs Listen Labs Validation

  • "Modern Prestige Beauty" category framing — Panel B tests this head-to-head vs. Premium Clean, Expert Beauty, and Skin-First. The winning frame shapes all skincare messaging.
  • Bobbi Brown dependency in skincare — Panel A's transfer test (Q4-Q7) reveals if JRB skincare has identity beyond Bobbi. If not, the skincare launch needs to build it.
  • Premium perception gap drivers — Panel B's open-ended Q2 ("what makes a brand worth paying more?") will reveal whether it's packaging, ingredients, experience, or story.
  • Age perception from visuals — Panel B Q12 tests whether non-customers think the brand is "for someone my age." If 35-44 year olds say no, skincare packaging needs to signal younger.
  • Which description commands premium? — Panel B Q5 vs Q6 tests which positioning is interesting vs. which commands premium pricing. They may differ.

Needs Bobbi / Team Input

  • SPF format decision — Standalone vs. built into moisturizer vs. tinted. Bobbi's perspective on how SPF integrates with her prep philosophy.
  • Miracle Cream reformulation scope — How different can the texture be without losing the "Miracle Cream" loyalists? Is a 2.0 or a line extension better?
  • Cleanser portfolio flavor — The 3-tier concept needs Bobbi's input on what "deep clean" vs. "gentle" means in her world.
  • Serum strategy — Is the Multivitamin Serum reformulation enough, or should JRB have a hero active serum (Vitamin C? Peptides? Retinol?)
  • Body category decision — Body Cream ($107K) and Body Oil ($185K) are small. Keep as extended line or divest to focus on face?
  • "Expert Skin Prep" positioning — Does this language resonate with how Bobbi thinks about skincare? The tagline needs her endorsement to be authentic.

Needs More Data

  • Skincare customer retention — What % of skincare buyers repurchase? How does this compare to makeup?
  • Cross-sell data — Do skincare buyers also buy makeup? What's the skincare→makeup bridge in actual purchase behavior?
  • Skincare-only customers — How many customers buy ONLY skincare, not makeup? What does that segment look like?
  • Subscription/replenishment opportunity — Skincare is inherently replenishable. Should JRB offer auto-replenish for core skincare?
