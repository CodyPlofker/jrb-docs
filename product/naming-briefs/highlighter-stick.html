Key Finding

The highlighter stick category is dominated by "glow" language — Glow, Luminizer, Illuminator, and Radiance appear in over half of competitor names. Meanwhile, no major competitor uses JRB's conversational or "The [Product]" naming patterns in this category, leaving wide open space for a name that sounds like something Bobbi would actually say.

Strategic Takeaways
Opportunity

"Glow" Is the New "Moisture" — Avoid It

6 of 12 competitors use "glow," "luminizer," "illuminator," or "radiance." Merit owns "Day Glow," RMS owns "Living Luminizer," Charlotte Tilbury owns "Beauty Light Wand." Any variation drops JRB into a sea of sameness.

Advantage

Stick Format = Naming Advantage

Most competitors put "stick" or "stix" in the name. JRB doesn't need to — the format speaks for itself. This frees the name to do something more interesting, the way "Miracle Balm" never says "multi-use."

White Space

The Bobbi Voice Is Wide Open

Zero competitors use conversational, Bobbi-style naming for their highlighters. This is JRB's strongest move — the same energy as "What The Foundation" applied to highlighter.

Positioning Deep Dive
Westman Atelier
Lit Up Highlight Stick
$48 Stick
"Clean luxury gel in a stick — prestige positioning for the editorial glow crowd."
A translucent gel formula with Vitis Vita Grape Extract (claimed to firm, boost moisture, and detoxify). Positions as the highest-end stick highlighter in the category at $48 full / $26 mini. Free of silicones, parabens, PEGs, phthalates, and synthetic fragrances. Vulnerable because the luxury MUA positioning (Gucci Westman's credentials) alienates the everyday woman who just wants to look good without thinking about it — JRB's core customer.
Translucent gel formula Glassy finish Grape extract (firming) No silicones/parabens
Merit
Day Glow Highlighting Balm
$30 Balm
"The minimalist's highlighter — dewy glow with zero sparkle, for the curated vanity crowd."
JRB's closest positioning competitor. Leads with plant-based squalane (hydration, elasticity), olive fruit oil, and a phytosterol/amino acid blend to "minimize moisture loss and support skin barrier." Positioned as "a sheer, lightweight moisturizing highlighter made for daytime wear that visibly illuminates for a dewy glow with zero sparkle." Vegan, recyclable packaging. The difference: Merit talks to a 28-year-old who curates her vanity for Instagram. JRB talks to a 45-year-old who wants to look like she got more sleep.
Zero sparkle Plant-based squalane Daytime wear Skin barrier support
Fenty Beauty
Match Stix Shimmer Skinstick
$29 Stick
"Magnetized, long-wear shimmer in 10 shades — the opposite of 'natural glow.'"
A cream-to-powder formula that's "weightless, blendable, and designed to be layered." Magnetized stick with 10 universally flattering shades. Claims longwear and extremely lightweight. Free of gluten, parabens, phthalates. Fenty owns the "stick highlighter" format in younger consumers' minds, but plays in a completely different register — visible shimmer, not natural glow. Not a direct positioning threat to JRB because the customer profiles barely overlap.
Cream-to-powder 10 shades Magnetized Longwear
RMS Beauty
Living Luminizer
$42 Pot
"The OG clean beauty highlighter — cult classic, minimal formula, hasn't evolved."
Built on certified organic raw coconut oil, castor seed oil (fatty acids, anti-inflammatory), and full-spectrum vitamin E from Spanish sunflowers. Claims a "translucent satin-pearl glow" that's "never greasy or glittery" with an "un-made-up appearance." Non-GMO, non-nano, cruelty-free. At $42, it's proven you can do clean + glow. JRB's opportunity: RMS hasn't evolved. The pot format feels dated compared to stick convenience, and their brand lacks the conversational, approachable energy JRB brings.
Organic coconut oil Satin-pearl glow Un-made-up look Non-nano
Charlotte Tilbury
Hollywood Beauty Light Wand
$42 Wand
"Hollywood glamour in a tube — one sells every 8 seconds, all aspiration."
Claims a "Hollywood effect glow" via proprietary "glow gel technology" with lindera extract for a high-gloss finish. 12ml in 7 shades (golds to rosy pinks). CT reportedly sells one every 8 seconds — massive viral success. But there's criticism about product volume for the price. JRB's counterposition is powerful: Bobbi would say "you don't need Hollywood lighting, you need good light." The anti-CT message practically writes itself.
Glow gel technology Lindera extract 7 shades 1 sold every 8 seconds
Rare Beauty
Positive Light Liquid Luminizer
$22 Liquid
"Botanical self-care glow at $22 — emotional positioning, not product expertise."
Formula features a botanical blend of lotus, gardenia, and white water lily (soothe and nourish). Claims a "silky, second-skin formula" that "lasts all day without fading, creasing, or settling into fine lines" and "layers over makeup without disturbing what's already there." At $22, it's the most affordable in the set. Clean at Sephora. "Positive Light" is an emotional positioning play, not a product descriptor. JRB doesn't compete on price or emotional wellness — it competes on expertise and ease.
Lotus + gardenia + water lily Second-skin formula No creasing/settling Clean at Sephora

White Space — Naming Directions
1

Conversational / Bobbi Voice

The widest-open lane. No competitor highlighter uses this approach. Think names with the energy of "Just A Sec" or "Sparkle On Top" — casual, confident, sounds like advice. This direction would be the most differentiated name in the entire category.

2

"The [Product]" — Definitive Simplicity

Only NARS plays here with "The Multiple" (which is about multi-use, not highlighting). A confident "The [something]" name would stand out against all the "Glow" and "Luminizer" names by being refreshingly direct.

3

Franchise Extension — Shimmer Family

JRB already owns "Shimmer" (Shimmer Brick, Shimmer Face Oil). A highlighter stick could extend this franchise. Risk: could feel too tied to Shimmer Brick, and "shimmer" might imply more sparkle than a natural-glow product delivers.

Notes for Review

The Conversational framework dominated this product — it produced the most JRB-feeling names and sits in the widest white space. Top picks are Catch Light, Nice Touch, and Good Light because they nail the Bobbi voice while being completely differentiated. The Franchise Extension framework (Shimmer Stick) is the safest path but doesn't have the personality of the conversational names.
