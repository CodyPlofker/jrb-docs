Section 09

7 Key Tensions

Where data sources conflict or create design tradeoffs.

1
Dashboard vs Storefront
Returning customers want efficiency (new arrivals, promos, nav). Prospects need trust and orientation (brand identity, reviews, curated paths).
Resolution: Above-fold serves both (product grid + trust bar). Below-fold diverges: product-discovery for returners, proof-building for prospects. Trust signals benefit everyone.
2
Bobbi: High Demand, Low Active Engagement
45.1% say Bobbi content is missing. 4.20/5 importance. But heatmap shows scattered engagement on the Bobbi section, and only 4% of N=51 cite founder video as a trust builder.
Resolution: Bobbi is a "vitamin, not a painkiller." Don't make Bobbi a standalone dead section. Integrate her INTO product context: "Founded by Bobbi Brown" in trust bar, Bobbi's pick tags, a focused two-column founder section with clear differentiation messaging.
3
Age Representation vs Brand Aspirationality
65+ cohort is least satisfied (6.7% fine). But younger prospects find the brand "boring." Moms report daughters seeing JRB as "mom's makeup."
Resolution: Cast across ages 35-65 with real skin. Bobbi as generational bridge. Don't skew old OR young.
4
Quiz/Shade Finder: Can't Find It, Don't Trust It
17.6% say quiz is hard to find, but 0% of first actions are looking for it. 4/23 prospects found it felt like a marketing funnel.
Resolution: Don't make quiz a homepage hero. Surface "Send a Selfie" and put shade tools contextually near product displays. Consider ILIA model: "Find My Shade" as a nav link.
5
Product Organization: Categories vs Concern vs Looks
37.4% go to nav (categories). 39.6% want skin-concern organization. Prospects want curated "looks."
Resolution: Offer multiple pathways. Category nav for directed shoppers, "Shop by Concern" for researchers, improved product grids for browsers.
6
Promotions: High Priority but Brand-Risky
81.3% rank promos top-3. But this is a loyal-customer sample. Charlotte Tilbury (prospect benchmark) leads with product story, not discounts.
Resolution: Promo bar is sufficient. Don't make promotions the homepage hero. New arrivals and product content lead; promos serve as secondary pull.
7
Editorial: Customers Reject It, Prospects Need Something
Ranked dead last (7.44/8). Zero heatmap engagement. But 5/23 prospects couldn't figure out what differentiates JRB.
Resolution: Don't create a "brand story" section. Weave differentiation into every element: "76K+ Reviews" as trust signal, "Founded by Bobbi Brown" as credential, "Clean Formulations" as product descriptor. Let brand emerge from product experience.

Section 01

Core Tension: Dashboard vs Storefront

The homepage must serve two fundamentally different jobs simultaneously. Returning customers want a dashboard (what's new, what's on sale, take me there fast). Prospects need a storefront (who are you, why should I trust you, help me discover).

Both Audiences
Returning
Prospects
Shared: Products, promos, nav
Returning: New arrivals, Bobbi, restock
Prospects: Trust, founder story, proof

The Design Principle

Sections that serve both audiences go above the fold. Sections that primarily serve one audience use progressive disclosure — returners get what they want quickly (products, nav), while prospects who scroll deeper find the proof they need (trust, founder, social proof).

Trust signals benefit everyone. A trust bar doesn't slow down returners but dramatically helps prospects.

Section 01

Testing Approach: Progressive Convertibles Stack

Each variation adds to the previous one, creating a cumulative stack. This lets us isolate the incremental value of each new element while measuring the compounding effect of the full redesign.

Why Progressive Stacking?

  • Isolates incremental value: If V2 beats V1, we know the trust bar + social proof specifically drove the lift
  • Answers the compounding question: Does the full experience (V4) compound, or do V1-V3 capture most of the value?
  • De-risks implementation: If V1 alone wins, ship V1 without building V2-V4
  • Informed by heatmap: We're replacing confirmed dead zones (editorial, Get the Look) with evidence-backed alternatives

Section 03

Key Question: What Goes in the Featured Products Slot?

The Featured Products position is the single hottest spot on the homepage. What we put there matters enormously.

The Dilemma

Three candidates for the hottest slot:

  • Current Featured Products — currently drives the most engagement
  • New Arrivals — #1 customer demand (94.5% top-3)
  • Bestsellers — safest for prospects (social proof baked in)

Recommendation: In V1, test New Arrivals in the hot Featured Products position. In V2+, keep Featured Products in the hot position and add New Arrivals as a separate section lower on the page. This tests whether New Arrivals in the prime position outperforms the current curation, while V2+ tests New Arrivals as an additive section.

Section 04

Outcome Interpretation

Six scenarios and what they tell us.

Scenario What It Means Action
V1 wins, V2-V4 flat New Arrivals alone captures most of the value. Trust signals and redesign are nice-to-have but don't drive incremental revenue. Ship V1. Deprioritize trust/redesign work. Focus on keeping New Arrivals fresh and dynamic.
V2 wins, V3-V4 flat Trust bar + social proof + New Arrivals is the sweet spot. Bobbi section and full redesign don't add incremental revenue. Ship V2. Bobbi is brand-building only (keep for equity, don't prioritize for conversion). Full redesign not needed.
V3 wins, V4 flat Bobbi adds measurable lift. The full redesign (collections + concern nav) doesn't compound further. Ship V3. Bobbi IS a conversion lever, not just brand. Skip the expensive collections/concern redesign.
V4 wins biggest The full experience compounds. Each element builds on the previous. The whole is greater than the sum of parts. Ship V4. The investment in the full redesign is justified. The homepage was significantly under-optimized.
Control wins Stated preference does not equal revealed preference. Customers say they want these changes but don't respond to them with their wallets. Re-examine assumptions. Look at segment-level data (new vs returning may diverge). Consider execution quality as a confound.
Results vary by segment New visitors may prefer V2-V4 (trust signals help), while returners prefer V1 (they just want products). Or mobile vs desktop may diverge. Consider personalized experiences: V2+ for prospects, V1 for returners. Or device-specific optimizations.
Section 05

Implementation Notes

Platform

Run via Intelligems Convertibles (homepage A/B testing tool). Convertibles supports content injection and section-level manipulation without requiring theme changes for each variant.

Traffic Allocation

  • Control: 20%
  • V1: 20%
  • V2: 20%
  • V3: 20%
  • V4: 20%

Primary Metric

Revenue per session. Secondary: conversion rate, AOV, add-to-cart rate, bounce rate.

Sample Size

With ~205K mobile sessions over 12 days (heatmap data period), we get ~17K sessions/day. At 20% allocation per variant, that's ~3.4K sessions/day/variant. To detect a 5% lift at 95% confidence (assuming 2% base conversion rate), we need approximately 15K sessions per variant, or about 5 days of traffic. Run for a minimum of 14 days to capture full weekly cycles.

V3 Bobbi founder section (two-column redesign) 1 day incremental

Section 06

Risk Mitigation

Risk Probability Mitigation
Novelty effect — new content gets temporary attention that fades Medium Run for 3-4 weeks minimum. Compare week 1 vs weeks 3-4 performance.
Execution quality confound — poor design of new sections masks concept value Medium Build to production quality, not prototype quality. QA across devices. Have design review before launch.
Page load impact — additional sections slow page Low-Medium Lazy-load below-fold sections. Monitor Core Web Vitals throughout test. Use Convertibles' built-in injection (no full page rebuilds).
Interaction effects — V4 results are confounded by too many changes Medium The progressive stack design handles this — V4 is just V3 + more. If V3 wins and V4 is flat, we know the additional elements didn't help.
Segment masking — aggregate results hide segment-level divergence High Pre-define segments before launch (see Segment Analysis Plan). Analyze new vs returning separately.
Holiday/promo interference Low Check promotional calendar before launch. Avoid overlapping with major site-wide sales events.
Section 07

Segment Analysis Plan

Pre-define all segments before launch. Do not mine for segments after the fact.

Primary Segments

Segment Why It Matters Expected Behavior
New vs Returning visitors The core tension (dashboard vs storefront). Trust signals in V2-V3 should help new visitors more. New visitors: higher lift in V2+. Returning: highest lift in V1.
Mobile vs Desktop 70% of traffic is mobile. Layout changes impact mobile differently. Desktop users are more likely to look for bestsellers/new (53.8%). Mobile: trust bar matters more (less screen to evaluate trust). Desktop: products-first matters more.
Direct vs Paid vs Organic Direct traffic is mostly returning customers. Paid traffic is mostly prospects. Organic is mixed. Paid traffic: higher lift in V2-V4 (these visitors need the most convincing).
Landing page: homepage vs other Only homepage-entry sessions should be analyzed. Visitors who enter on PDP and navigate to homepage have different intent. Homepage-first sessions are the clean signal. Filter out PDP-first navigation.

Secondary Segments (Directional Only)

  • AOV buckets: Do trust signals increase AOV for first-time buyers?
  • Product category purchased: Does New Arrivals prominence change the product mix?
  • Session depth: Do V2-V4 visitors view more pages (proof of exploration)?
  • Time on page: Does the redesign increase or decrease homepage dwell time?

Option: Staged Launch

If the full 5-way test is too complex to build simultaneously, consider launching in two phases:

  • Phase 1: Control vs V1 vs V2 (2-3 weeks). Quick to build, answers the highest-priority questions.
  • Phase 2: Winning variation vs V3 vs V4 (2-3 weeks). Only build V3-V4 if Phase 1 shows a winner.

This reduces upfront build effort by ~50% while still getting to the same answer.
