1. How V1 Works Today

The Shade AI tool launched as an MVP that lets customers upload a selfie and receive personalized shade recommendations across JRB's complexion line. Here's the architecture:

Customer uploads selfie
→

→
Determines Skin Tone (8 levels) + Undertone (3 types)
→
Lookup table maps to product shades

V1 Training Data

The V1 system prompt was built from ~85 manually-read CX shade consultations. These were found by searching Richpanel ticket subjects for keywords like "shade recommendation." The CX team's matching patterns were distilled into the lookup table above and embedded into the Claude system prompt.

Built-in Nuances

  • Lighting adjustment — warm/golden lighting → adjust cooler; fluorescent → adjust warmer
  • Redness/rosacea — redness ≠ cool undertone; use Bronze/Sunkissed MB instead of Dusty Rose/Flushed
  • Olive undertone detection — distinguishes cool-olive from warm-olive
  • Borderline rule — lean lighter (better light than too dark for JRB)
  • Beige is modal — ~40% of matches; default to Beige when in doubt between Light and Beige
  • Match to neck — if face lighter than neck due to sunscreen

The core limitation: V1 was trained on only ~85 consultations found via subject-line search. We've since discovered there are 30,697 shade-match tagged conversations in Richpanel — a 360x larger dataset than what V1 was built on.

3. How We Analyzed It

1

Richpanel Tag Discovery

Scanned all 155,700 Richpanel tickets and discovered the shade-match tag — applied to 30,697 conversations. The original V1 only found 85 by searching subject lines, missing 99.7% of the data.

2

Full Conversation Extraction

Extracted all 30,697 shade-match conversations via the Richpanel API. Parsed each one for: customer form data (name, email, current foundation, products requested, selfie URL), agent messages, shade recommendations, and follow-up feedback signals.

3

Junip Review Analysis

Analyzed all 381,099 Junip reviews. Filtered to 220,923 shade-product reviews. Text-mined for shade signals ("too dark," "too light," "too pink"). Mapped 35,903 reviews with self-reported skin tone survey answers.

4

Cross-Reference (The Key Step)

Matched customer emails between Richpanel and Junip. Found 2,735 customers who got a CX shade recommendation AND later reviewed the product. For each: compared the recommended shade vs. what they bought, and whether they rated it 4-5 stars or 1-2 stars.

5

Outcome Classification

For each matched customer, classified: exact match (bought exactly what was recommended), adjacent match (within 1-2 shades), or different shade (bought something else). Then correlated with satisfaction.

5. V2 Recommendations

High Impact

1. Rebalance Miracle Balm Defaults

Problem: Dusty Rose is recommended 44% of the time but scores 76% positive — the lowest of all major MB shades. It's the default for every skin tone in the V1 "All-Over Tint" row.

Change: Default MB tint recommendations by undertone instead of universally defaulting to Dusty Rose:

  • Cool undertone → Dusty Rose (keep, it works for cool)
  • Neutral undertone → Flushed (86% positive) or Chic (92%)
  • Warm undertone → Bronze (80%) or Sunkissed (82%)

Also recommend Miami Beach more broadly — it scores 92% positive across all skin tones.

High Impact

2. De-prioritize Magic Hour and Happy Hour

Problem: Magic Hour scores 68% positive and Happy Hour scores 60% — by far the weakest MB shades. Happy Hour has 24-26% low ratings across ALL skin tones.

Change: Remove Magic Hour and Happy Hour from default "Highlighter" recommendations. Replace with Golden Hour (universally strong) or recommend as secondary options only.

High Impact

3. Shift Face Pencil Recommendations 1 Shade Lighter

Problem: Adjacent matches (customers who bought 1-2 shades off from the recommendation) have 87% positive satisfaction vs 80% for exact matches. Combined with complaint patterns showing "too dark" more than "too light," this suggests V1 recommendations skew slightly dark.

Change: When the AI is between two Face Pencil shades, strongly favor the lighter option. The existing "lean lighter" rule in the prompt should be strengthened.

Medium Impact

4. Add Undertone Differentiation for FP 05-08

Problem: Shades 05-08 get contradictory feedback — both "too light" and "too dark" on the same shade. This means the same shade is being recommended to cool AND warm undertone customers in the same skin tone bracket.

Change: For Light and Light-Medium skin tones, split Face Pencil recommendations by undertone:

  • Cool undertone → favor the lower shade number (e.g., 05 over 07)
  • Warm undertone → favor the higher shade number (e.g., 07 over 05)
Medium Impact

5. Fix WTF Deep for Dark Skin

Problem: WTF Deep has 33% low ratings for Dark skin customers. Almond (4.70 avg) and Cinnamon (4.86 avg) significantly outperform it.

Change: For Dark skin, recommend WTF Almond or Cinnamon first, not Deep. Only recommend Deep for Deep (the darkest) skin tone.

Medium Impact

6. Build Foundation Cross-Reference Lookup

Problem: Customers often provide their current foundation brand/shade. V1 ignores this. Our data shows clear mappings (e.g., MAC NC40 → FP 15-17, Bobbi Brown Warm Beige → FP 4-6).

Change: When a customer mentions their foundation, use a validated lookup table (built from 1,400+ cross-referenced consultations) to anchor the recommendation, then fine-tune with the selfie analysis.

Medium Impact

7. Fix Specific Product Corrections

  • Face Pencil 01 for Fair skin gets "too light" (70 mentions) → recommend FP 02 instead
  • Pinky Bronze MB gets "too dark" from Fair/Light → don't recommend for Fair/Light
  • Flushed MB gets "too pink" (161 mentions) → add caveat about pink sensitivity
  • Neutralizer Light Peach gets "too pink" for Light skin → recommend Fair Pink instead
  • Tinted Face Powder Dark has 40% low ratings for Medium-Dark → recommend Medium instead

6. Next Steps

1

Update System Prompt & Lookup Tables

Implement the 7 recommendations above in the V2 system prompt and shade-data.ts lookup tables. Deploy to a Vercel preview URL.

2

Side-by-Side Testing

Run 50+ sample selfies through both V1 and V2. Compare recommendations. Spot-check against the ground-truth dataset to validate V2 is more accurate.

3

Feedback Loop

V1 already collects feedback via the /api/feedback endpoint (stored in Upstash Redis). Monitor V2 feedback to measure improvement. Target: move from 80% positive to 90%+ positive satisfaction.

4

CC-Tag Dataset (Bonus)
