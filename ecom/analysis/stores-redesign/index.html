3. Customer & Brand Context

1,609 Junip reviews mention "store" and 268 mention "in person." Richpanel CX data confirms shade matching as the #1 ticket driver. Customer intelligence from 28 studies across 5 platforms.

Why Customers Visit Stores (from Junip reviews)

Shade Matching (#1 reason)
Shade uncertainty is the #1 friction point across ALL data. 46% of prospects cite shade concern. Customers call stores for shade advice, get matched in person, and convert.
"I called a store in Dallas... they said I should buy ivory. I trusted them since I knew I could return it. I am THRILLED!!!"
Makeup Lessons & Applications
Makeup lessons are described as "life changing", "empowering", "exceeded expectations." Staff show techniques that drive additional product purchases.
"I walked in to the Montclair store and immediately felt comfortable. I didn't feel too old and didn't feel too inexperienced."
Converting Hesitant Online Buyers
Store visits convert people who are afraid to order online. The physical experience bridges the trust gap that the website can't.
"I haven't worn makeup in 10+ years... I was afraid to order online. A store opened near me so I went for it!"
Demand for More Locations
Customers actively ask for stores in their cities. Pent-up demand exists in markets JRB hasn't reached yet.
"JonesRoad Beauty is the best. Just wish you had a few more store fronts around the country (Midwest or southwest)!"

4. Design Principles

5 research-backed principles to guide the redesign. Every design decision should trace back to one of these.

Principle 1
Lead with the experience, not the address
Data: Apple, Bonobos, Away all market what you'll DO at the store. Junip reviews confirm: shade matching, makeup lessons ("life changing"), and staff expertise are the real reasons people visit. 19.6% of non-purchasers say trying in person would convert them.
The hero should sell free shade matching, consultations, makeup lessons, and events — not just list 12 addresses.
Principle 2
Show the stores, don't illustrate them
Data: Away and Everlane use real per-store photography — each store feels like a destination. JRB's illustrations are charming but hide what the experience looks like. Junip reviewers describe visits with emotional language — they want to preview the vibe.
Replace illustrations with real store photography (interiors + exteriors). Each store should feel distinct, like a place worth visiting.
Principle 3
At 12 stores, browse — don't search
Data: Everlane (11 stores) uses a pure visual grid, no map. Away (~15) uses flagship + grid. Key competitive insight: <15 stores = visual grid/editorial approach. JRB's current linear scroll forces you through ALL 12 sequentially.
Compact visual grid that lets users scan all 12 at once. No map needed yet — introduce one at 20-25 stores.
Principle 4
Make shade matching the hero CTA
Data: Shade uncertainty is #1 friction across 10+ sources. 46% of prospects cite shade concern. 24% of CX tickets are shade-related. Junip reviews confirm in-store shade matching is the moment of conversion. The retail store IS the solution the site can't provide.
Position stores as THE shade-matching destination. "Book a Shade Match" should be as prominent as general appointment booking.
Principle 5
Build anticipation for what's coming
Data: 12 to 24 by year end. No competitor does "coming soon" well (whitespace gap). Junip reviews show customers asking for stores in their cities — pent-up demand waiting to be captured.
Add a "Coming Soon" section with upcoming cities. Capture interest with email signup by market.
5. Recommended Approach

What to build, which patterns to follow, and where to exploit whitespace.

Page Structure (top to bottom)

SECTION 01
Brand Hero
Full-bleed lifestyle photo of a real store interior or in-store experience. Headline focuses on the EXPERIENCE, not the location. Subhead sells shade matching + consultations. Two CTAs: "Book a Shade Match" (primary) + "Find a Store" (secondary). Pattern: Reformation's full-bleed hero + Away's casual "Visit us IRL" tone.
SECTION 02
Experience Highlights
3-4 cards showing what you can DO at a JRB store: Free Shade Matching, Makeup Lessons, Private Events, Try Everything. Each with an icon/image and short copy. Pattern: Apple's in-store experience marketing.
SECTION 03
Visual Store Grid
3-column grid of all 12 stores. Each card: real store photo (interior/exterior) + neighborhood name + city/state. Click/tap to expand details (hours, address, phone, directions, booking). Pattern: Everlane's pure visual grid + Away's per-store photography.
SECTION 04
Coming Soon
Upcoming store openings by city. "We're growing" headline with 2026 expansion cities. Email capture: "Get notified when we open near you." Pattern: JRB whitespace — nobody does this.
SECTION 05
Social Proof (optional)
2-3 customer quotes from Junip reviews about store experiences. Real quotes that sell the in-store experience to hesitant online shoppers. Pattern: JRB whitespace.

Key Design Decisions

Photography > Illustration
Real store photos for the grid. The illustrations are charming and could live on as a secondary design element (e.g., detail page headers), but the primary browsing experience needs real photos.
Grid > Scroll
3-column grid on desktop, 2-column on tablet, 1-column stack on mobile. All 12 stores visible without scrolling through 12 viewports. Click to expand for details.
Experience-first copy
Lead with what you'll DO, not where you'll GO. "Find your perfect shade" > "Find a store." "Book a consultation" > "Get directions."
Nav presence needed
Stores should be accessible from the main nav or utility bar — not buried in the footer. Either under "More" or as a standalone utility link like "Find Your Shade."

Concept Spectrum (for Phase 4)

Concept A: Medium Risk
Keep current structure but upgrade: real photos in same layout, add experience section above store list, add "Coming Soon." Familiar but improved.
Concept B: Bold
Full rethink: experience-first hero, visual grid, shade-matching as the page's purpose. Store as destination, not directory. Away + Everlane hybrid.
Concept C: Safe
Best execution of the current pattern: keep illustrations, compact the layout into a scannable grid, add "Book a Shade Match" CTA. Minimum viable upgrade.
