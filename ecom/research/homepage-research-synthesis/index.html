2. Qualitative Insights

A. ListenLabs Prospect Study (March 2026) — 23 Non-Customers

Study details: 23 screen recordings of non-customers browsing JRB for the first time. Ages 35-64. Recruited from online beauty shoppers. NOTE: 10/23 (43%) could not load the website due to technical issues, limiting usable data to ~13 participants who actually browsed the site.

Homepage-Specific Findings

Finding: Homepage impression is polarizing among prospects
There is no consistent first impression. The site reads differently to different people:

"The page seems oriented towards people who are much younger than me, like maybe Gen Z." — Participant 0005, age 46
"The Jones Road website feels very, almost like very clinical and very antiseptic — almost like it doesn't feel welcoming or warm." — Participant 0005, age 46
"I like that it's kind of giving a retro vibe, kind of like a '90s vibe. I like that a lot." — Participant 0043, age 36
"First impressions: don't like Jones Road. I'm already aware of them and I find everything about the brand boring and unnecessary." — Participant 0138, age 35

Finding: Homepage feels "not for me" to 45+ women (3/23)
Multiple prospects over 45 felt the homepage visually skewed young, signaling it was not for their demographic.

"The page seems oriented towards people who are much younger than me, like maybe Gen Z." — Participant 0005, age 46

Finding: Brand differentiation is unclear on first visit (5/23)
Prospects who don't already know Bobbi Brown see JRB as indistinguishable from other beauty brands. The "clean, strategic, high-grade formulations" differentiator is invisible on the homepage.

"I don't really see what differentiates Jones Road from many of the other premium beauty products in that same category. There are just so many to choose from." — Participant 0005, age 46
"I guess I still don't know who you are. I know your name. I know what products you sell. But who are you, Jones Road?" — Participant 0121, age 49
"I would definitely have 'clean, strategic, high-grade formulations.' I would have that. And maybe I missed it, so make it where I can't miss it." — Participant 0121, age 49

Finding: Pop-up email gate creates hostile first impression (4/23)
For prospects with zero brand affinity, the first interaction with JRB is a popup asking for their email. Several called it annoying — the first impression is transactional rather than inspirational.

Finding: Charlotte Tilbury is the direct competitor in prospects' minds
When asked to compare, prospects repeatedly cited Charlotte Tilbury — specifically its warm, welcoming site aesthetic, entertaining copy, and clear "glow" messaging.

4. New vs Repeat — What Returners Need vs What Prospects Need

This is the most critical segmentation for homepage design. The data reveals fundamentally different jobs-to-be-done.

What RETURNING CUSTOMERS need (Typeform + Advisory Board):

  1. "What's new?" — New Arrivals is the #1 priority (94.5% top-3, 49.5% say it's missing)
  2. Efficient navigation — 37.4% go straight to nav; they know what they want
  3. Promotions and deals — 81.3% rank promos in their top 3
  4. Bobbi content — 45.1% want more Bobbi on the homepage; 4.20/5 importance rating
  5. Recognition — "I would love a website that remembers who I am" — repeat visitors don't want to be treated as strangers
  6. NOT the quiz — 0% of first actions are looking for the quiz; the shade quiz popup annoys long-time users

What PROSPECTS need (ListenLabs):

  1. "Who is Jones Road?" — Brand differentiation is unclear to 5/23 prospects
  2. "Is this for me?" — Homepage feels Gen Z to 45+ prospects; age representation matters
  3. Social proof / trust signals — 16/23 cite reviews as primary trust signal; "76K reviews" needs to be visible
  4. Warmth, not clinical — Charlotte Tilbury's welcoming aesthetic is cited as the benchmark; JRB reads "clinical and antiseptic" to some
  5. Product discovery via looks, not categories — Prospects don't know what to buy; they need curated entry points
  6. Clean beauty credential visibility — 9/23 care about ingredients; the clean positioning is buried
  7. NO popup as first interaction — 4/23 found the email popup off-putting as a first impression

The Tension:

Returning customers want the homepage to be a dashboard (what's new, what's on sale, take me to what I want). Prospects need the homepage to be a storefront (who are you, why should I trust you, help me figure out what to buy). The homepage must serve both without compromising either.

6. Competitive Context — What Competitors Do on Their Homepages

ILIA Beauty (Primary Competitor for Homepage UX)

  • "FIND MY SHADE" as dedicated top-level nav link
  • "REWARDS" visible in nav
  • Full-width hero for new product launches
  • Clean category nav: Shop All, Bestsellers, Complexion, Eye, Lip + Cheek, Skincare, Sets, Tools
  • GWP promo bar

MERIT Beauty

  • Shade categorization by tone family ("The Pops", "The Neutrals")
  • Model-per-shade gallery thumbnails
  • Bundle cross-sell above fold ("5 Minute Morning")
  • Clean badge formatting: "Clean at MERIT, Vegan, Cruelty Free, Fragrance-free"
  • 30-Day Shade Match Promise

Rare Beauty

  • "SHADE FINDER" as top-level nav link
  • "NEW" badges on shade swatches
  • "Limited Edition" vs "Core" categorization (urgency + permanence)
  • Social impact messaging near ATC

Charlotte Tilbury (Prospect-Cited Benchmark)

  • Warm, welcoming site aesthetic (cited by multiple ListenLabs prospects)
  • Entertaining copy
  • Clear "glow" positioning
  • Strong editorial/lifestyle photography

What Every Competitor Has That JRB Lacks on Homepage:

  1. Shade Finder / "Find My Shade" in main nav
  2. Clear product categories with visible subcategories
  3. Prominent review count / social proof
  4. Clean/ingredient badges
  5. Rewards/loyalty visibility

What JRB Has That No Competitor Can Match:

  1. Bobbi Brown — the most credible founder in clean beauty
  2. 76,000+ reviews — more than Rare Beauty (4.8K), MERIT (6.9K), Rhode (16K) combined
  3. $21.59/oz value — 2-8x more product per ounce than any competitor
  4. "Send a Selfie" personal shade matching — a unique human service
  5. 4 uses per product — unmatched versatility (but must be communicated carefully to prospects)

7. Key Tensions — Where Data Sources Conflict or Create Tradeoffs

Tension 1: New Customer vs Returning Customer Homepage

  • Returning customers (91 Typeform responses) want: new arrivals, promos, efficient nav, Bobbi content
  • Prospects (23 ListenLabs recordings) need: brand identity, trust signals, curated entry points, warmth
  • Resolution: Personalized experience (logged-in vs. first-visit) or above/below-fold split where above-fold serves both (new arrivals + trust bar) and below-fold diverges

Tension 2: Bobbi Content — High Demand but Not Top Priority

  • 45.1% say Bobbi content is MISSING from the homepage
  • But only 33% rank it in their top 3 priorities
  • Bobbi importance is 4.20/5 for purchase decisions
  • For prospects who don't know Bobbi, the founder story needs more prominence
  • Resolution: Bobbi is a "vitamin, not a painkiller" — important for brand health but shouldn't displace conversion-driving elements. Integrate Bobbi INTO product sections (e.g., "Bobbi's Pick" tags, short video clips within product cards) rather than as a standalone section

Tension 3: Age Representation vs Brand Aspirationality

  • 65+ cohort is the least satisfied with the homepage (only 6.7% say it's fine)
  • Prospect 0138 (age 35) found the brand "boring" — younger prospects want energy
  • Multiple moms report daughters seeing JRB as "mom's makeup" before trying it
  • Advisory Board: "Bobbi herself is the only thing that bridges the gap between generations"
  • Resolution: Cast across ages, don't skew old OR young. Bobbi as the generational bridge. Models 35-65 with real skin, not just age-diverse casting with perfect skin.

Tension 4: Quiz/Shade Finder — Customers Can't Find It, Prospects Don't Trust It

  • 17.6% of customers say the quiz should be easier to find on the homepage
  • But 0% of customers' FIRST action is looking for the quiz
  • 4/23 prospects who used the quiz found it felt like a marketing funnel
  • Resolution: Don't make the quiz a homepage hero. Instead, surface "Send a Selfie" (trusted by both groups) and put shade tools contextually near product displays, not as a standalone homepage CTA.

Tension 5: Product Organization — Categories vs Skin Concern vs Curated Looks

  • 37.4% go straight to nav menu — they want product categories
  • 39.6% say skin-type organization is missing
  • Prospects want curated "looks" and guided entry points
  • Resolution: Offer multiple pathways: category nav for directed shoppers, "Shop by Concern" for researchers, and "Get the Look" for browsers/prospects. Not one-size-fits-all.

Tension 6: Promotions — High Priority for Customers, Risky for Brand

  • 81.3% rank promos in top 3 for homepage
  • But this is a loyal-customer sample who actively seeks deals
  • Over-indexing on promos can cheapen brand perception for prospects
  • Charlotte Tilbury (prospect benchmark) leads with product story, not discounts
  • Resolution: Promo bar is sufficient — don't make promotions the homepage hero. Let new arrivals and product content lead; promos serve as secondary pull.

Tension 7: Editorial Content — Customers Reject It, But Brand Needs It for Prospects

  • Editorial/brand story ranked dead last (avg 7.44 out of 8) by customers
  • But 5/23 prospects couldn't figure out who JRB is or what differentiates them
  • Resolution: Don't create a "brand story" section. Instead, weave differentiation into every element: "76K+ reviews" as a trust signal, "Founded by Bobbi Brown" as a product credential, "Clean, strategic formulations" as a product descriptor. Let the brand story emerge from the product experience, not from a separate editorial block.

8. Recommended Priorities — Stack-Ranked List of What the Homepage Must Do

Ranked by combined frequency of demand (Typeform quantitative), conversion impact (ListenLabs qualitative), and cross-source triangulation strength.

Tier 1 Non-Negotiable (Data overwhelming, conversion-critical)

1. Prominent, Always-Fresh "New Arrivals" Section

  • Evidence: 94.5% top-3 priority ranking; 49.5% say it's missing; 63.3% of 65+ cohort demands it; 100% of Restockers put it in top 3
  • Must be above the fold, clearly labeled, and genuinely updated (not the same products for weeks)
  • This is the single highest-demand homepage element across all segments

2. Clear, Fast Product Category Navigation

  • Evidence: 37.4% first action = nav menu; 39.6% top-3 ranking; 52.2% of 45-54 go to nav first
  • Open-ended complaint: "I absolutely hate the full width drop downs that cover the whole screen"
  • Category nav must show products directly, not require an extra "Shop All" click
  • Consider: restructure by concern/type alongside traditional categories

3. Visible Promotions / Current Offers

  • Evidence: 81.3% top-3 ranking; second-highest priority across all shopper types
  • Promo bar or banner — doesn't need to be the hero, but must be visible and current

4. Trust Signals for First-Time Visitors

  • Evidence: 16/23 prospects cite reviews as primary trust; 10/23 search for return policy; "76K reviews" is JRB's strongest competitive proof point
  • Must include: review count, return policy, free shipping threshold, clean beauty badges
  • ILIA and MERIT both surface trust signals prominently — JRB doesn't

Tier 2 High Priority (Strong data, significant impact)

5. Bobbi Brown Content Integration

  • Evidence: 45.1% say it's missing; 4.20/5 importance; 3,334 review mentions of Bobbi; +0.3-0.5 star lift when Bobbi is referenced
  • NOT a standalone editorial section (editorial ranks last). Instead: Bobbi's picks, short video clips, "Bobbi recommends" tags on products
  • For prospects: "Founded by Bobbi Brown" must be prominent — 5/23 didn't know who she is

6. "Shop by Concern" / Skin-Type Organization

  • Evidence: 39.6% say it's missing; 53.3% of Researchers want it; 83.3% of 35-44 want it
  • Serves both navigation (find products for MY concern) and education (learn what products address MY issue)
  • Also serves prospects who don't know the product catalog

7. Age-Diverse Visual Representation

  • Evidence: 18-reaction Advisory Board comment (highest engagement); 3/23 prospects felt excluded; 65+ cohort least satisfied (6.7% say homepage is fine)
  • Models 35-65 with real skin. Don't remove young models — add diversity across ages.
  • This is both a brand signal and a conversion lever: "I see someone like me using this"

8. Bestsellers Display

  • Evidence: 34.1% top-3 ranking; 31.9% first action = look for bestsellers; 41% of Browsers put it in top 3
  • Especially important for new visitors who don't know the catalog
  • Should show reviews/ratings on tiles (ListenLabs: 71% have price/value concerns)

Tier 3 Important Supporting Elements

9. Product Demo Videos

  • Evidence: 27.5% say it's missing; Researchers most interested (40%); ListenLabs: quality over quantity
  • Short, high-quality Bobbi demos — NOT autoplay carousels
  • ONE video per product feature, user-initiated play

10. "Where to Start" Guide / New Customer Path

  • Evidence: 16.5% say it's missing; 5/23 prospects had "who is Jones Road?" confusion
  • Curated entry point for first-time visitors — quiz-free, education-first
  • "New to JRB? Start here" with bestseller recommendations + Bobbi video intro

11. Shade Finder Access (Contextual, Not Hero)

  • Evidence: 17.6% want it easier to find; BUT 0% first action = look for quiz; 4/23 prospects distrust quiz
  • Don't make quiz a homepage hero. Surface "Send a Selfie" + shade tools contextually near product displays
  • Consider ILIA/MERIT model: "Find My Shade" as a nav link, not a homepage section

12. Risk-Reduction Signals for Acquisition

  • Evidence: 12/23 prospects cite price-as-risk; 9/23 want minis/samples
  • "Try the Mini" callout near featured products
  • Visible return policy: "Not your shade? Free returns."

Tier 4 Do NOT Prioritize on Homepage

13. Editorial / Brand Story Section

  • Evidence: Ranked dead last (avg 7.44 out of 8); 0 people put it #1; 1.1% top-3
  • Brand story should emerge from product experience, not a standalone section
  • Weave "Founded by Bobbi Brown" and "Clean, strategic formulations" into product sections

14. Quiz as Homepage Hero

  • Evidence: 0% first action; only 6.6% top-3; prospects distrust it
  • Move quiz to contextual placement (nav link, product pages)

15. Heavy Discount / Sale Emphasis

  • Evidence: Only 2.2% first action = look for what's on sale
  • Promos matter, but don't lead with discounts — lead with products

Limitations

  1. Typeform sample skews old and loyal: 93.4% are 45+, zero under-35 respondents. All are Advisory Board members (super-loyal). Prospect needs are under-represented in quantitative data.
  2. ListenLabs prospect sample is small: Only 13 of 23 participants actually browsed the site. Directional, not statistically significant.
  3. Older ListenLabs study is 9 months old: The site has evolved since June 2025. Findings should be treated as background context, not current state.
  4. No behavioral analytics in this synthesis: This brief contains stated preferences and observed behavior from studies, but not click-through, scroll-depth, or conversion data from actual homepage traffic. GA4/Hotjar data would strengthen or challenge these findings.
  5. Both ListenLabs URLs required authentication and could not be re-pulled fresh. Data in this synthesis draws from the locally-saved ListenLabs prospect analysis and the existing customer insights file, both of which contain extracted findings from those studies.
