1. What the Tool Is

The AI Shade Matching Tool is a web app where a customer uploads a selfie and gets personalized shade recommendations across the JRB complexion line: What The Foundation, Face Pencil, Neutralizer, Miracle Balm, and Tinted Face Powder.

Customer takes selfie
→
Claude Vision AI
analyzes skin tone + undertone
→
Shade lookup tables
map tone to products
→
Personalized recommendations

The AI classifies the selfie into one of 8 skin tones (Pale, Fair, Light, Light-Medium, Medium, Medium-Dark, Dark, Deep) and one of 3 undertones (Cool, Warm, Neutral). That combination then maps to specific shade recommendations for each product.

Tech Stack Next.js + TypeScript, deployed on Vercel. The AI model is Claude Sonnet 4 (Anthropic's vision model). Shade lookup data is stored in static TypeScript files. Feedback is collected via Upstash Redis.

2. How V1 Was Built

V1 was built in two layers, each from a different data source:

Layer 1: The System Prompt (from Richpanel CX Data)

The core AI instructions — what tells the model how to read a face and match it to a shade — were written from 97+ real shade matching consultations pulled from the Richpanel CX ticketing system over 18 months (January 2025 – March 2026).

These were actual customer conversations where someone emailed the JRB CX team with a selfie and an agent responded with shade recommendations. We extracted the patterns from these real expert recommendations:

  • How agents assess a bare-face selfie (jawline + forehead, compare face to neck)
  • The WTF shade anchors the entire recommendation — everything else maps from it
  • Edge cases: rosacea doesn't mean cool undertone, olive can be cool-olive or warm-olive, borderline cases lean lighter
  • Face Pencil under-eye is always 1-2 shades lighter than face
  • Lighting adjustments for warm/cool/fluorescent environments

The consultations were organized into date-range batches and reviewed for consistent patterns:

Layer 2: The Shade Lookup Tables (from Quiz Logic)

The product-specific mappings — which exact shade of Face Pencil or Miracle Balm to recommend for each skin tone — were built from two existing JRB quiz data sources:

  • Miracle Balm Quiz: shade-recommendation-copy-table.csv — maps each skin tone to MB shades by usage (Blush, Bronzer, Highlighter, Tint, Glow)
  • Complexion Quiz: quiz-logic.csv — maps skin tone + undertone + coverage preference to WTF, JETM, Face Pencil, and Neutralizer recommendations

These were the same logic tables that power the existing shade quizzes on jonesroadbeauty.com. The AI tool essentially combines CX expert judgment (how to read a face) with quiz logic (what to recommend).

How the two layers interact The system prompt also contains a shade-to-product mapping table (from CX data) that acts as a cross-check. When Claude Vision says "Light-Medium, Warm," the prompt says "that maps to WTF Beige, Face Pencil 08-10, Neutralizer Light Peachy Pink" — and the lookup table in code confirms with exact product details.

3. V1 Strengths and Weaknesses

Where V1 is strong

  • Fair through Light-Medium skin — the bulk of CX training data. 97+ consultations, all in this range. The prompt has rich, nuanced rules for these tones.
  • Undertone detection — rosacea handling, olive detection, warm vs cool indicators are well-documented in CX data.
  • WTF recommendations — the core product mapping is battle-tested across hundreds of CX consults.
  • Edge case rules — "lean lighter when in doubt," seasonal adjustments, face-vs-neck matching.

Where V1 is weak

Critical: Zero dark-tone training data We scanned 1,200+ Richpanel CX conversations across 15 months. We found zero completed shade matching consultations for Medium-Dark, Dark, or Deep skin tones. Not a single one. The darkest recommendation found in the entire dataset was Medium Honey (Face Pencil 10).

Why the gap exists:

  • JRB's CX email channel skews overwhelmingly fair-to-medium (audience is 65+ and primarily lighter-skinned)
  • Darker-skinned customers may use the quiz, retail locations, or simply not reach out for shade help via email
  • The shade finder quiz funnels most customers before they email — those who email tend to be quiz failures, which skew light

What this means for V1: For Dark and Deep skin tones, the tool is essentially guessing based on the quiz logic tables, not validated by real CX expert consultations. The Miracle Balm recommendations for Dark/Deep also lack the nuanced "this shade reads too pink on deeper tones" guidance that exists for lighter shades.

4. V2 Research: Junip Review Analysis

V2 RESEARCH New data source to validate and improve shade logic

The Idea

Junip collects a Skin Tone survey answer on every review (Fair, Light, Light-Medium, Medium, Medium-Dark, Dark, Deep). Each review is also tied to the exact product + shade the customer purchased, plus their rating and review text.

That means we have real customers telling us: "I have Light-Medium skin, I bought WTF Beige, and I rated it 5 stars." Or: "I have Dark skin, I bought Deep, and I rated it 1 star because it was too dark."

This is ground truth data at a scale the CX tickets could never reach.

How the Analysis Worked

Step 1: Satisfaction Matrix

For every combination of (Skin Tone + Product + Shade), we calculated: number of reviews, average rating, percentage of 5-star reviews, percentage of 1-2 star reviews, and recommendation rate. This tells us which shade actually works for which skin tone.

Step 2: Text Mining for Shade Signals

We searched all 201K shade product review texts for specific language:

  • Direction signals: "too dark" (found 1,000+), "too light" (found 1,200+), "too pink" (found 400+), "too orange" (found 200+)
  • Correction signals: "exchanged for," "switched to," "should have gotten"
  • Validation signals: "perfect match," "perfect shade," "spot on"
  • Tool signals: "shade finder," "shade quiz" (534 mentions)

Step 3: Cross-Reference

Compared the review-validated recommendations (what shade happy customers with X skin tone actually bought) against the current tool's logic (what shade the tool would recommend for X skin tone). Where they diverge = improvement opportunity.

5. Biggest Findings

High Priority — Clear Mismatches

1. WTF for Dark Skin: Wrong Shade Recommended

HIGH PRIORITY

Current tool maps Dark skin to "Rich" / shade 18+. Review data shows Almond (4.70 avg) and Cinnamon (4.86 avg) are the real winners. Deep has a 3.17 average with 33% low ratings.

Evidence: 29 reviews from self-reported Dark skin customers. Almond = 70% 5-star, Cinnamon = 86% 5-star, Deep = 17% 5-star.

"It is difficult to choose the correct color of foundations online. I chose the Deep Fonce Foundation which was too dark for my dark skin and did not complement my skin tone." — Dark skin customer, WTF Deep, 1 star

2. Miracle Balm Happy Hour: Weak Across All Tones

HIGH PRIORITY

Currently recommended as Highlighter for Pale and Fair. Happy Hour has 24-26% low ratings across every skin tone — the weakest shade in the entire Miracle Balm range.

Evidence: Fair = 25% low (n=294), Light = 26% low (n=453), Light-Medium = 24% low (n=466). By comparison, Miami Beach has 1-7% low ratings across all tones.

3. Miracle Balm Pinky Bronze: Too Dark for Fair/Light

HIGH PRIORITY

Currently recommended as Bronzer for Fair. Pinky Bronze has 116 "too dark" mentions across reviews, concentrated in Fair and Light-Medium customers.

Evidence: 11 "too dark" mentions from Fair, 10 from Light, 28 from Light-Medium.

"I have fair skin and like Dusty Rose Miracle Balm. Thought Pinky Bronze would bring a rose warmth but found it to be too Orange-Brown and look unnatural." — Fair skin customer, Pinky Bronze, 1 star

4. Tinted Face Powder Dark: Bad for Medium-Dark

HIGH PRIORITY

The Dark shade of Tinted Face Powder has 40% low ratings from Medium-Dark customers. The Medium shade works dramatically better (4.69 avg, 0% low ratings).

Evidence: n=5 for Dark (small sample but clear signal), n=13 for Medium (strong).

"As a woman of color, we have various shades of skin. I found it difficult to find a tinted powder that fit my color... Maybe produce more shades for women of color." — Medium skin customer, Tinted Face Powder Dark, 1 star

Medium Priority — Refinements

5. Flushed is Polarizing (161 "Too Pink" Mentions)

MEDIUM

Flushed is currently the primary Blush recommendation for Fair through Medium. It works well for many (4.48 avg for Fair) but has the most "too pink" complaints in the entire dataset. Especially problematic for warm undertones.

6. Miami Beach is Under-Recommended

MEDIUM

Currently listed as just an "Alt" blush option. Miami Beach has 79-94% five-star ratings across every skin tone. It's the single most universally loved shade in the MB range.

Evidence: Fair=82% 5-star, Light=79%, Light-Medium=74%, Medium=83%, Medium-Dark=94%.

7. Face Pencil 01 Too Light for Fair Skin

MEDIUM

70 "too light" mentions for Shade 01. Even Fair-skinned customers report it doesn't provide enough coverage. Shade 02 is the better default for Fair.

"Way too pale despite the quiz telling me to buy it! I have fair skin and red tones to my face, and shade 2 was perfect." — Fair skin customer, Face Pencil Shade 01, 2 stars

8. Neutralizer: Medium Customers Prefer Going Lighter

MEDIUM

Current tool recommends Medium Peachy Pink for Medium skin. But Light Peachy Pink (4.36 avg, n=83) outperforms Medium Peach (4.14 avg, n=71) with Medium-skinned customers.

6. Proposed V2 Changes

All changes would be made on a new branch, deployed to a separate Vercel preview URL. The current live tool stays untouched. We'd test the same selfies on both versions and compare outputs side-by-side before merging.

What changes

There are two files in the tool that control recommendations:

FileWhat It DoesWhat Changes
route.ts
System Prompt		 Instructions to Claude Vision — how to read a face, shade mapping table, edge case rules Add a "Review-Validated Corrections" section with the findings above. Additive — existing CX rules stay.
shade-data.ts
Lookup Tables		 Hardcoded product recommendations per skin tone Update specific shade defaults where review data shows a clear better option (e.g., Dark skin WTF mapping).

What does NOT change

  • The AI vision model itself (Claude Sonnet 4) — no retraining
  • How selfies are captured or processed
  • The UI/UX of the results page
  • The feedback collection system
  • Any recommendation that the review data confirms is working
