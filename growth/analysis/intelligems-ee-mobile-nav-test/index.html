Key Finding — Currently Deployed

V2 (Bestsellers Row) is a statistically significant conversion loser — but is live at 100% traffic

V2 reduces CVR by 3.17% (p2bc 0.022 — statistically significant) and checkout begin rate by 3.42% (p2bc 0.003). The bestsellers row creates browse-mode behavior: users click more products and visit more PDPs (+6.9%), but fewer reach checkout. V1 (PLP category links only, no bestsellers) is the cleanest variant — directionally flat on revenue with a massive +52.7% collection navigation lift that doesn't hurt conversion. V2 has been ramped to 100% allocation, likely before revenue signal became clear.

Critical Paradox
V2 simultaneously drives more browsing (more PDP views, more collection views, more hamburger clicks) and less converting. This is a classic "browse mode" trap — increased engagement without purchase intent. The bestsellers row may be triggering discovery behavior at a moment when users had higher purchase intent.

V2's #1 next destination is the homepage itself — users are entering the nav, clicking a bestseller, then returning to the homepage. This back-and-forth suggests the bestsellers row is interrupting the navigation decision, not completing it. V1 sends users directly to collections (the intended behavior). NRPV Comparison
Collection View Rate — All Variants

The most statistically significant effect of this test: all nav variants dramatically increase collection page navigation. This is expected — but V2 proves navigation lift alone does not equal revenue lift.

Key Learnings
  • Bestsellers rows in navigation create browse mode, not purchase intent V2 added a horizontal bestsellers product row to the nav. It drove +6.87% more PDP views (p2bc 1.0) and +3.08% more hamburger clicks (p2bc 1.0) — all engagement metrics went up. But CVR dropped 3.17% (p2bc 0.022) and checkout begin dropped 3.42% (p2bc 0.003). Navigation is a wayfinding tool; injecting product content into it shifts user mindset from "I'm going somewhere" to "I'm browsing," which kills purchase intent.
  • PLP category links in nav are safely neutral — not a revenue risk V1 (PLP links only, no bestsellers) increased collection view rate by +52.7% (p2bc 1.0) — the highest of all variants — without hurting CVR (+0.68%, p2bc 0.65 — noise), ATC, or checkout. The nav change successfully routes more users to collections without cannibalizing conversion. This is the safe foundational layer.
  • V2 is live at 100% and losing revenue every day V2 was ramped to 100% allocation — likely because navigation metrics and engagement looked positive before revenue signal was conclusive. At 298K control visitors over 14 days (~21K/day) with a $1.682 baseline NRPV, V2's −2.67% NRPV drag costs approximately $350–$400 per day in net revenue. Immediate action to revert or replace with V1 is recommended.
  • The "Shop All" CTA variant (V3) makes no difference V3 swapped "Shop All Primary" for "Shop All Secondary" in the bestsellers row. Metrics are virtually identical to V1 (CVR +0.74% vs +0.68%, NRPV −0.10% vs +0.40%). The difference is not measurable. The Shop All CTA swap is not a lever worth testing further.
Future Test Hypotheses
01
Ship V1 as Permanent Foundation
V1 delivers +52.7% collection navigation with zero revenue cost. It should be shipped permanently (not as a test). The learnings from V2 confirm the base nav improvement is safe — the bestsellers row was the problem, not the PLP links.
Action: Revert V2 → ship V1 to 100%
02
Test Bestsellers on PDP or Cart, Not Nav
The bestsellers row drove genuine product interest (+6.87% PDP views). The problem was placement inside the nav at the decision-making moment. Test a bestsellers row module on the PDP sidebar, cart drawer, or a dedicated collection page — where browse mode is appropriate and high-intent users are already in product discovery.
Primary metric: ATC rate → CVR | Why: Same product content, different timing in journey
03
Test Intent-Gated Nav Merchandising
If merchandising in nav is desired, test showing bestsellers only after the user has been on site 2+ minutes or has visited a PDP (returning user intent signal). This surfaces product content to browse-mode users who have already engaged, rather than interrupting navigate-intent users.
Primary metric: CVR (mobile) | Hypothesis: Behavioral targeting preserves nav utility for high-intent users
Traffic Allocation Note

V2 received approximately 2× the traffic of other variants (575,808 vs ~297,000–298,300) because it was ramped to 100% allocation mid-test. This means V2 has statistically stronger confidence intervals than V1/V3. However, even with higher power, V2's CVR and checkout begin losses remain statistically significant (p2bc 0.022 and 0.003). The ramp did not help V2 — it only confirmed the signal more definitively.

V1 and V3 remain at noise-level on revenue metrics — not because they are losers, but because the signal is genuinely small and the test would need longer run time or higher traffic to reach significance on NRPV. V1's directional positive trend (+0.40% NRPV, +0.68% CVR) and its zero-cost collection navigation improvement make it the recommended path forward.
