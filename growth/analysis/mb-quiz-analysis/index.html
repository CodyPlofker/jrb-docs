Key Findings

Interstitial Screens Are Quiz Killers

Every screen where the user doesn't need to do something has a massive drop-off. Testimonials (63%, 58%), confirmations (79%, 52%), and the review screen (64%) act as natural exit points. Users see content that isn't asking them to engage, and close the tab. These 5 screens alone account for the majority of lost completions.

The Undertone Section Is a Double Problem

It's losing the most people and may not be adding much value:

  • 67% of users say they DON'T know their undertone
  • Of those who "discover" it, 56% land on neutral — the catch-all default
  • The flow spans 3 screens with cumulative drop-off stacking
  • The confirmation screen alone has a 79% drop-off — the worst in the entire quiz

Result Distribution Is Highly Concentrated

73% of all completers get just 2 shades (Dusty Rose 42%, Flushed 31%). This could be legitimate given the audience skews light/fair/pale (71% of skin tone responses), but it's worth auditing whether the algorithm is over-indexing on these shades or if the quiz truly can't differentiate within that cohort.

Actual Questions Perform Well

Skin type, skin concerns, makeup routine, age, and plan-to-use all have 0–4% drop-off. Users are happy to answer questions — they just don't want to be interrupted with non-interactive content between them.

Recommendations

  1. Remove or inline the testimonial screens. Two screens with 63% and 58% drop-off that add no quiz logic. If you need social proof, embed it as a small element within a question screen — not as a standalone step that interrupts the flow.
  2. Kill the "Review Answers" screen. 64% drop-off for zero utility. Users want results, not a summary of what they already told you.
  3. Remove the "Pre-Results" summary. 52% drop-off right before the finish line. Go straight from email capture to the loader/results.
  4. Compress the undertone flow from 3 screens to 1. A single visual undertone selector with inline education would replace the question → select/discover → confirmation chain. Given most people don't know their undertone and most results land on "neutral," evaluate whether this signal is even moving the needle on shade recommendations.
  5. Consider moving email capture earlier. Currently at step 17, only ~21K emails captured from 70K starts. Moving it right after the core questions (skin type, concerns, routine) would significantly increase capture volume.
  6. Audit the shade algorithm. 73% of results landing on 2 shades either means the algorithm isn't granular enough, or the quiz questions aren't creating enough differentiation to separate Dusty Rose from Flushed candidates.

Bottom Line

The actual quiz questions perform excellently — people are happy to answer. The problem is everything between the questions. Testimonials, confirmations, reviews, and summaries are acting as exit ramps. Stripping those interstitials out could realistically lift completion rate from 48% to 60–65% and increase email captures proportionally — potentially an additional 8,000–12,000 emails per month.
