4. Evidence Deep-Dives

Top 4 fixes examined in detail — data, customer quotes, competitive benchmark.

Step 1 — Proof on Product Tiles

The Problem

Featured product tiles are the #1 organic click destination on the homepage (tiles 1-3 = ~24K combined clicks, the highest non-popup activity). Yet they show only product name and image. Price and star ratings are hidden until the user clicks through to the PDP — adding friction to the fastest-intent path on the site.

The Evidence

  • 63% of users notice featured products first (Homepage Eval)
  • 71% cite price/value concerns (ListenLabs, n=24)
  • 26.4% of actual MB buyers almost didn't purchase due to price
  • Heatmap: tiles 1-3 are the #1 on-page organic click destination
  • Customer research explicit ask: "proof on tiles" (ratings, price)
"I want to see reviews before I scroll down."
"I feel like with makeup, especially when you're buying it online, it's really helpful to have a before and after picture."

Competitive Benchmark

Merit Beauty: Homepage product tiles show price, star count, and a brief descriptor inline. Rhode Skin: Product cards on homepage include star rating + review count + price. Rare Beauty: Featured products show price, shade count, and star rating on the grid card. JRB is the outlier — no price or rating on homepage tiles is non-standard among premium DTC beauty brands.

Recommendation

Add star rating (e.g., 4.8 ★), review count, and price to all featured product tiles. Keep visual hierarchy: product name > image > [rating + price]. Optionally add a "Best Seller" or shade count badge. This is a single template change — Ease: 8.

Step 2 — Hero UVP + Above-Fold CTA

The Problem

Despite being the first element users see, the hero generates almost no measurable click data in the heatmap top 25. There is no hero CTA button in the top 25 clicks at all — it simply isn't being clicked. With 67s average time on page and 35% avg scroll, the hero is not motivating users to scroll or engage.

The Evidence

  • Hero CTA is absent from the top 25 clicks (heatmap data)
  • Only 17% of users notice the hero/main banner at all
  • Average scroll depth: ~35% of page — many users bounce without engaging below fold
  • Clean beauty messaging noticed by only 17% — not being communicated by the hero effectively
  • Past test: JRB01 (Homepage Banner Copy) ran Sep–Nov 2025, result unclear
"The hero section is beautiful but I didn't know where to start. I ended up looking at the products below it."

Competitive Benchmark

Merit Beauty: Hero headline is benefit-led ("Effortless Beauty") with a direct Shop CTA prominently placed. Ilia: Hero features a specific product name + UVP ("Skin-first makeup") + Shop button. Rhode: Hero is minimal but includes one clear CTA button ("Shop Rhode"). All three brands drive clicks from the hero; JRB’s hero appears decorative rather than directive.

Recommendation

Test a hero with: (1) benefit-led headline ("Skin-first makeup. Actually works."), (2) a single prominent Shop CTA button in the hero content area, (3) a sub-line addressing the top UVP ("Clean ingredients. 28 shades. Dermatologist approved."). On mobile, ensure the CTA is visible without scrolling. Ease: 7.

Step 3 — Social Proof Bar Above Featured Products

The Problem

Social proof (press logos, review aggregate, testimonials) is currently located at fold 3 or below. The scroll data shows approximately 65% of homepage visitors never reach fold 3. That means the majority of traffic never sees JRB’s most powerful trust signals before they leave or convert via other means.

The Evidence

  • ~65% of sessions do not reach fold 3 (scroll depth analysis)
  • JRB10 (Social Proof on Homepage) ran Sep–Nov 2025 — inconclusive but the test confirms this hypothesis was worth exploring
  • Customer research: "I want to see reviews before I scroll down"
  • Research finding: "Reviews showing zero kills conversion"
  • Press logos are JRB’s strongest external validator (Vogue, NYT, etc.) and are currently invisible to 65% of homepage traffic
"I trust Ulta that has a lot of different brands more than just a main brand — I'd want to see what other outlets say."

Competitive Benchmark

Merit Beauty: Press logo bar appears in fold 2, directly below hero. Rare Beauty: Sephora badges + press mentions appear in fold 2. Ilia: Aggregate review rating ("4.8 stars, 50,000+ reviews") surfaces in fold 1 as a hero sub-element. Industry standard is to place social proof within the first 2 folds.

Recommendation

Move the existing press logo bar (jrb11-press-logo-bar.liquid) above the featured products section. Additionally, add a 1-line trust statement: "240,000+ five-star reviews. As seen in Vogue, NYT, and Forbes." Ease: 7 (Liquid section reorder, no new dev).

Step 4 — Shade Finder / Quiz CTA Prominence

The Problem

Shade anxiety is the most cited and most-confirmed friction point across the entire JRB customer intelligence system — 10 separate data sources confirm it. The Find My Shade quiz is one of JRB’s best CRO tools (quiz pages JRB18 + JRB21 both won) but it is buried below fold 4 on the homepage, unreachable by ~65% of visitors.

The Evidence

  • 46% of prospects cite shade/undertone uncertainty as a blocker
  • 7,048 CX tickets/quarter (24%) relate to shade selection
  • 50.5% score 1–3 out of 10 on shade confidence (Website Feedback, n=91)
  • 26.9% of 1,185 non-purchasers cite shade uncertainty as their barrier
  • JRB18 + JRB21 (quiz page optimizations) both won — quiz users convert
  • JRB24 (Shade popup on cart) washed — late-funnel shade help doesn’t work; early-funnel does
"The shade matching part was confusing — I wasn’t sure which undertone I am."
"78% want swatches on 3+ skin tones" — Customer Intelligence (10+ source confirmation)

Competitive Benchmark

Ilia: "Find Your Shade" CTA is a persistent element in the featured products grid and in the hero. Rare Beauty: "Shade Finder" is linked from the main nav AND from the complexion product category. Merit: Shade selector embedded directly in the product tile. JRB’s shade quiz is well-built (quiz pages won) but is not promoted effectively from the homepage.

Recommendation

Add a "Find Your Shade" CTA block between the featured products grid and fold 3. Minimal design: dark background tile with headline "Not sure which shade? We’ll find your match in 60 seconds." + button linking to the shade quiz. Ease: 6 (new section, low complexity).

4.5 Competitive Audit — Homepage

Live audit of 10 competitor homepages — April 14, 2026. Six beauty brands (Merit, ILIA, Glossier, Kosas, Rare Beauty, Saie) and four best-in-class non-beauty DTC brands (Away, Mejuri, On Running, Aesop). Captured via Chrome MCP DOM inspection. Findings are used to pressure-test and extend the fix list in Section 3.

Universal Patterns — Table Stakes (7+ brands)
  • Hero → Featured Products → Category Grid (10/10). Every homepage leads with a single hero tile, then a bestsellers/featured-product grid within 1–2 sections. No one leads with lifestyle or brand-world content above fold.
  • One primary CTA above the fold (10/10). Each hero reduces to one action. Never 3+ competing CTAs.
  • Prices visible on homepage product tiles (10/10). Zero brands hide price behind a click. This is the strongest universal pattern in the set.
  • Category entry points directly below featured products (9/10). Makeup / Skincare / Face / Eye / Lip split appears on every beauty site and most non-beauty analogs.
  • Newsletter/VIP capture: delayed popup + persistent CTA (8/10). 3–8s delayed modal plus a header-persistent “Get 10%” or “Become a VIP”. JRB’s Alia popup matches this pattern.
  • Footer-heavy trust / sustainability block (8/10). Certifications, mission, tree counters live late-page — not above fold.
Common Patterns — Worth Considering (4–6 brands)
  • Quiz / shade-finder as a dedicated mid-page card (6/10). Merit, ILIA, Rare Beauty, Kosas, On Running, Away all embed a quiz entry mid-page. Never primary nav, never above-fold-as-hero.
  • Curated Sets / Bundle row (6/10). Merit “The Sets”, ILIA “Sets”, Rare Beauty trios, Away “Bundle & Save”, Aesop duos, Mejuri gift sets. Standard AOV lever.
  • Star ratings + review count on product tiles (5/10 beauty). Merit, ILIA, Kosas, Rare Beauty show it. ILIA’s “4.5 · 15,144 Reviews” callout is the gold standard.
  • Video in hero or secondary section (5/10). Glossier (2), Kosas (8), Away (7), Rare Beauty (6), ILIA. Aesop and Merit stay photo-only for premium feel.
  • Value-prop / utility bar (shipping / returns) (5/10). Mejuri right after hero; Away as dedicated 4-up late-page; Kosas / Saie in announcement bar.
  • Announcement bar above nav (6/10). Rotating promo/new-launch/shipping text. Away, Saie, Mejuri, Merit, Rare Beauty.
Differentiators — Opportunities (1–3 brands)
  • Two-panel “Skin First / Makeup Second” split — Glossier only. Mid-page row with two giant category tiles + one-word headlines. Simpler than a 6-category grid and directly mirrors JRB’s “skin-first makeup” positioning.
  • Editorial / mission statement as a standalone mid-page block — Kosas (“Beauty is who you are to yourself. No one else.”), Aesop (“A neighbourly presence”). One paragraph + image. Adds brand voice without slowing commerce.
  • Explicit “Reasons to Get [Brand]” 4-up trust block — Away. Personalize / Free Shipping / Engineered by Experts / Visit Us IRL. Works as a pre-footer objection-handler.
  • Aggregate review count callout near featured products — ILIA. “4.5 / 15,144 Reviews” anchors the grid instead of requiring reviews on every tile.
  • Services row (Stores / Piercing / Cleaning) — Mejuri. Elevates brand experience beyond product. Not directly applicable but the structural pattern (3-card “what we do beyond ship”) could become a JRB “Quiz / Shade Match / Sample” row.
Gaps / Whitespace — What Nobody Does
  • No brand leads with heavy lifestyle video or long-form storytelling above featured products. Hierarchy is uniformly product-first. JRB should not experiment against this.
  • No brand uses a review-wall or testimonial carousel as an early-page section. Reviews live on tiles or PDPs. Mild whitespace opportunity.
  • No brand audited leverages a founder-credential hero. Rare Beauty (Selena) is the closest but frames it as brand voice, not founder credential. Bobbi Brown’s credential is the single largest unused asset. A simple “Founded by Bobbi Brown” signature tile (not a long bio) is whitespace.
  • No brand shows UGC or before-afters as an above-fold section. UGC appears in late-page “@brand” rows only.
  • No brand ties the primary hero CTA to a quiz flow. Quiz is always a mid-page card. Validates the JRB approach of NOT making hero CTA = quiz.

Implications for JRB Homepage

Patterns that VALIDATE the existing fix list

  • Prices on product tiles — Universal (10/10). Not doing this is out-of-market. Reinforces Step 1 in Convertibles ladder and the “proof on tiles” ask from 71% of surveyed customers.
  • Single above-fold CTA — Universal (10/10). Matches brief’s Step 2.
  • Ratings / review counts on tiles — Common pattern (5/10 beauty). ILIA’s 15,144-review anchor is the gold standard. Reinforces Steps 1 + 5.
  • Quiz stays a mid-page card, not a nav item — 6/10 brands with quizzes place them mid-page. Validates existing shade-quiz card placement recommendation.

NEW opportunities surfaced by the audit

  • Add: Aggregate review callout above featured products (inspired by ILIA). A single “4.7 / 2.3M+ reviews” line hits the “proof on tiles” ask without per-tile review integration. ICE suggestion: Ease 8 — low-effort add to the fix list.
  • Add: “Reasons to Get JRB” 4-up trust block (inspired by Away). Clean formulas / Free shipping / Shade-match guarantee / Founded by Bobbi Brown. Sits between featured products and brand story. Suggested new fix-list item.
  • Add: Two-panel “Skin-first makeup” split (inspired by Glossier). Directly mirrors JRB positioning. Could augment or replace the current single category entry. Suggested as a new Convertibles variation path.
  • Add: Curated Sets row after featured products (6/10 brands). Standard AOV lever. Promotes existing kits/bundles.
  • Whitespace test (longer horizon): Founder-credential moment. No competitor leverages a founder of Bobbi’s caliber. A simple “Founded by Bobbi Brown” credential tile is unique whitespace — consistent with the Feb 2026 brand-positioning directive to extract Bobbi essence into JRB identity.

Noted but BLOCKED by JRB07 (active nav test)

  • Rotating announcement bar above nav — present on 6/10 brands. Queue as first post-JRB07 nav test.
  • Persistent “Get 10%” or “Become a VIP” header CTA — Mejuri, ILIA. Queue post-JRB07.

Patterns JRB should NOT copy

  • Mission-first hero (Saie). One-brand outlier. JRB data (17% notice hero vs 63% notice products) says product-led hero wins.
  • Zero-video, zero-reviews minimalism (Aesop). Only works at Aesop’s tier. JRB needs proof density, not reduction.
  • Heavy celebrity/campaign hero (Rare Beauty). JRB is Founder Mode (Bobbi), not celebrity collab. Direct copy would feel inauthentic.

8. Quick Reference Card

Top 5 Customer Barriers (Homepage-Relevant)

  1. Shade anxiety — 46% prospects, 7,048 CX tickets/qtr. Fix: Shade Finder CTA (Step 4).
  2. Price / value — 71% prospects, 26% buyers hesitated. Fix: Proof on tiles + mini module (Steps 1, 5).
  3. No proof above fold — 63% notice products first; no ratings visible. Fix: Proof on tiles + press bar (Steps 1, 3).
