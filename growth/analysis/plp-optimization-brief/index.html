Evidence Deep-Dives

Top 3 Fixes — Full Evidence Stack

For the three highest-priority fixes, this section documents the full evidence chain: heatmap data, customer research findings, customer quotes, and competitive benchmarks.

1
Ratings + Review Count on Product Tiles
ICE: 9 · 9 · 7 = 25

The Problem

JRB collection page tiles show product image, name, and price — but zero social proof. There are no star ratings, no review counts, and no "bestseller" signals. On a brand that relies on "earned trust" positioning, this creates a silent credibility gap at the discovery layer.

Quantified Customer Evidence

"I want to see reviews before I scroll down." — ListenLabs User Research, Jun 2025 Homepage Evaluation
"Reviews showing zero kills conversion" — documented in CRO knowledge base as a named pattern. Moving reviews below the fold on PDPs hurt conversions. — JRB CRO Knowledge Base, derived from Convertibles testing learnings

Heatmap Signal

No direct heatmap click on a "rating" element exists because ratings don't exist on tiles yet. However, the heavy tile-image swiping behavior (4K+ swipe-to-2nd-image events across 3 pages) suggests users are seeking additional product information at the tile level — ratings would be consumed in this discovery moment.

Competitive Benchmark

Rare Beauty
✓ Star rating (4.8★) + review count (1,091) visible on all collection tiles
Ilia Beauty
✓ Reviews prominently surfaced on product cards. "Skincare-grade" trust positioning backed by visible proof.
JRB (current)
✗ No ratings or review count visible on any collection tile. Users must click to PDP to see social proof.

Recommended Change

Add star rating (1 decimal, e.g. 4.7) + review count in parentheses below the product title on all collection tiles. Use Junip review data already integrated. Style: small text, star icon, lightweight — does not require redesign of tile card.

2
Price Prominent + Compare Price on Tiles
ICE: 8 · 9 · 8 = 25

The Problem

While price appears on tiles today, the implementation may not be prominent enough — especially for sale/compare pricing. When 71% of users want price visible and 26.4% of actual buyers almost didn't purchase due to price, every pixel of price signal matters. Compare price (strikethrough) and savings percentage ("Save 20%") should be tile-level features, not PDP-only.

Whitespace — What Nobody Does Well

Seven gaps in the audited set. Each is a candidate for JRB to own.

Gap 1
Explicit Kit Savings Math
  • Merit shows strikethrough ($156 → $133) but no "Save $23" math.
  • Tower 28 uses "Save $6" badges but doesn't break out value.
  • Nobody says "Save $23 vs. buying separately."
  • Kits CVR = 2.12% — this is the single clearest whitespace move.
Gap 2
Skin Concern / Persona Filters
  • Rare Beauty: Coverage + Finish (closest).
  • Kosas: shade family + undertone.
  • On: activity (Running, Hiking, Tennis).
  • Nobody in beauty filters by concern (aging, redness, dullness) — which is how 45+ customers actually shop.
Gap 3
Collection-Level Trust Bar
  • No brand added a trust strip above the grid with review averages or ingredient callouts specific to that collection.
  • Opportunity: "4.8★ average · Clean ingredients · Dermatologist tested" strip above Face collection.
Gap 4
Inline Video on Tile
  • Despite video being the dominant ad format in beauty, every tile used static images.
  • 3–5-second texture / application loop on hover would be a dramatic differentiator.
Gap 5
"Bought Together" Logic on PLP
  • No tile in the audit surfaced cross-sell or bundle logic at the grid level.
  • Opportunity on Kits collection: "Complete the routine — add Gentle Cleanser for $X more."
Gap 6
Velocity / Trending Proof
  • No "X bought this week" or "trending now" signal on any tile.
  • Cheap to ship, high FOMO lift, especially for sale / seasonal products.
Gap 7
Age / Life-Stage Matching
  • No brand explicitly matches products to age or life stage.
  • Given JRB's 45+ core and Bobbi positioning, "Best for mature skin" filter or tile badge is a natural fit.

Implications for JRB — Priority Adjustments

How the audit changes or validates the fix priorities in the table above.

1 Ratings — upgrade to Stars + Review Count (Rare Beauty model) Keep Step 1

Rare Beauty's ★★★★★ 1091 REVIEWS treatment is doing more work than stars alone. The review count (1091, 218, 436) is the credibility carrier — large, legible, visually anchored under the product name. Keep ratings as a Step 1 priority but specify the format as stars + full count, not stars + "(N)" in small type. ILIA's stars-only treatment is weaker.

Rare Beauty
✓ "★★★★★ 1091 REVIEWS" — count is prominent, hyperlinked.
ILIA
~ Stars visible, no count shown — weaker social proof.
Allbirds, Glossier, Kosas, Tower 28, Merit, Mejuri, On
✗ No stars on tile at all.
2 Shade indication — add "X SHADES" overlay as Step 1, swatch dots as Step 2 Split into two phases

Kosas's shade-count overlay ("38 SHADES" / "6 SHADES" text over the image) is a Step 1-effort move — a CSS overlay on product imagery, no tile image-swap backend. It delivers ~70% of the shade-visibility benefit. The full swatch-dot pattern with tile image-swap (Tower 28, Merit model) can come in Step 2.

Revised plan: Step 1 adds "X SHADES" text overlay on the image for any product with ≥ 2 shades. Step 2 replaces it with interactive swatch dots that swap the tile image.

3 Kit Savings Math — raise priority New Step 1 / 2 candidate

This is whitespace — no brand in the 10-brand set shows explicit "Save $X vs. buying separately" math on kit tiles. Given Kits CVR at 2.12% (44% below site average), this is the highest-leverage move on the weakest-performing collection. Merit shows strikethrough pricing ($156 → $133); Tower 28 uses "Save $6" generic badges. Nobody does the explicit math.

Proposal: Kits tiles get three additions — (a) "Save $23" badge top-right, (b) "Includes Miracle Balm, JETM + 3 more" subtitle, (c) 2nd tile image shows individual products laid out. Low-dev, on-brand, direct revenue logic.

4 Sub-category Pill Nav — validates the mobile-exit fix Keep Step 2

Seven of ten brands (Glossier, Kosas, Rare Beauty, Mejuri, On, Allbirds, ILIA) use horizontal pill-chip sub-nav above the grid. This directly addresses JRB's mobile-exit pattern (6,593 exits on Face; 5,424 on Kits). Users leaving to the hamburger menu and clicking "Shop All" are doing what a pill-chip nav would let them do in-place.

Proposal: Sticky horizontal pill-chip sub-nav above the grid, showing current sub-category + 4–6 related ones (e.g., on Face: Base · Lip · Cheek · Eyes · Brow · Tools). Kosas and Rare Beauty are the cleanest templates.

5 Third-party Trust — add Press / Dermatology badges on hero products (Step 3) New Step 3 candidate

ILIA overlays Allure / Byrdie award badges directly on product imagery. Tower 28 overlays National Eczema / Rosacea / Psoriasis Foundation certifications. Both are cheap to implement (static image badges) and lift credibility. JRB has an existing press kit and clean-beauty positioning — there is no reason Allure / Byrdie / Vogue badges shouldn't appear on top-selling product tiles.

6 Concern / Life-Stage Filters — fully untapped in beauty (Step 2 / 3) New Step 2 / 3 candidate

On Running's activity-based nav (Running, Hiking, Tennis) translates cleanly to beauty as concern-based nav (Dryness, Redness, Aging, Uneven tone, Sensitive). No brand in the audited set does this in beauty. Given JRB's 45+ customer research showing concern-driven shopping, this is a genuine whitespace move that reframes PLP around customer intent, not product type.

Design Vision

What the Optimal JRB PLP Looks Like

A description of the fully-optimized collection page with all fixes applied. This is the "best challenger" — the full-treatment variation that V3 represents.

Zone 1 — Above Grid
Collection Trust Bar + Filter Nav
  • Thin trust bar: "Clean ingredients · 4.8★ average · Free shipping $85+"
  • Category sub-nav tabs (existing) + "New Arrivals" and "Best Sellers" filter options
  • Sort by: Featured · Best Sellers · Price Low-High · New Arrivals
  • Skin concern filter: Dry skin · Aging · Uneven tone · Sensitive
  • Mobile: horizontal scrollable filter pills, always visible above grid
Zone 2 — Product Tile
Tile Card — Full Information Layer
  • Product image (swiper with 2nd lifestyle/on-skin image)
  • Badge (New / Bestseller / Award Winner / Sale) — top-left corner
  • Star rating (e.g., 4.7) + review count (e.g., 1,247 reviews) — below image
  • Product name — clear, full title
  • Shade swatch dots (for shade variants) — click to preview on tile
  • Price — prominent, compare price strikethrough + "Save X%" on sale items
  • Quick-Add CTA (single SKU products) — appears on hover/tap of tile
Zone 3 — Kits Specific
Kit Tile Enhancements
  • 2nd tile image shows individual products included in kit
  • Badges: "Save $X vs. buying separately" value callout
  • Quick-add available (kits are single-SKU, no shade selection needed)
  • Kit contents preview: "Includes Miracle Balm, JETM + 3 more" as subtitle text
Zone 4 — Mobile UX
Mobile-First Tile Grid
  • 2-column grid maintained (current, confirmed working)
  • Touch-friendly swatch dots: minimum 7mm × 7mm per Baymard standard
  • Sticky mini filter bar above grid on scroll
  • Rating + price always visible within first scroll on 375px viewport
  • No content above grid that increases scroll-to-product distance
What this version does not include: Major layout changes (no grid-to-list toggle, no sticky sidebar filters, no hero banner above grid). The Convertibles framework prioritizes additive changes to the tile card over structural page redesigns — consistent with the test history pattern that major redesigns underperform incremental improvements.
Convertibles Framework

Variation Ladder

The Convertibles approach builds the optimal version first, then tests by adding complexity in steps — or removes elements to isolate what's driving lift. V1 is the minimum viable improvement; V3 is the full treatment.

V1 — "Proof on Tiles"
Hypothesis: Adding ratings + ensuring price prominence moves the conversion needle without requiring shade-matching infrastructure
  • Add star rating (1 decimal) + review count below product title on all tiles (Pull from Junip)
  • Ensure price is visible, readable (14px+), and not truncated on mobile viewports
  • Show compare price strikethrough + "Save X%" badge on any discounted products

The lightest-lift combination of the two highest-confidence fixes. Should be testable with minimal dev work (likely CSS + liquid adjustments to tile card). If this doesn't move RPV, it tells us social proof and price clarity aren't the primary blocker — and we look deeper at shade/navigation friction.

V2 — "Shade + Signals"
Hypothesis: V1 gains + shade swatches + product badging increase both click-through rate and shade confidence — reducing returns and repeat sessions
  • Everything in V1 +
  • Shade swatch dots on tiles for all shade-variant products (tap to preview, image updates)
  • New / Bestseller / Award badges on qualifying tiles
  • Quick-Add CTA for single-SKU products (Kits priority) — appears on tile hover/tap

Adds the two highest-impact changes that require more development. Shade swatches address the #1 compound friction point (46% shade uncertainty + 7,048 CX tickets). Kits quick-add targets the worst-performing collection (2.12% GA4 CVR). If V2 beats V1, shade infrastructure is a confirmed lever.

V3 — "Full Discovery Layer"
Hypothesis: The full optimal PLP — proof + shades + filters + anchoring — achieves maximum CVR improvement by resolving all identified friction simultaneously
  • Everything in V2 +
  • Trust bar above grid: "Clean ingredients · 4.8★ average · Free shipping $85+"
  • Enhanced filters: New Arrivals + Best Sellers tabs + skin concern filter options
  • Kits tile: 2nd image shows kit contents (individual products); "Includes X, Y, Z" subtitle
  • Collection-level anchor content (Face + Kits): single-line curated headline above grid to reduce nav exits

The full-treatment build. Tests whether structural additions (filter expansion, trust bar, kit content preview) compound the gains from V1/V2 or dilute focus. If V3 underperforms V2, the structural additions are adding noise. If V3 beats V2, filter/anchoring is a real conversion driver.

Control — Current State
Baseline: current JRB collection page (no ratings on tiles, price as-is, no shade swatches, no quick-add, current filter tabs)
  • Product image + product name + price on tiles
  • Category sub-nav tabs above grid
  • Sort dropdown (price, featured, new)
  • Alia quiz widget (existing popup)
  • No ratings, no shade swatches, no badges, no quick-add

Keep all users on control during test ramp-up. Stop criteria: any variation down more than 5% RPV after 3 days should be paused. Minimum test duration: 2 weeks. Run on Shop All first (159K sessions/month) for fastest significance, then apply winner to Face + Kits.

Test Plan Notes

  • Primary metric: Revenue Per Visitor (RPV) — consistent with all JRB tests
  • Secondary metrics: Add-to-Cart rate, Collection-to-PDP CTR, Collection CVR
  • Launch sequence: V1 first (lowest risk, fastest to build). If winner, layer V2 on top. If V2 wins, layer V3.
  • Start page: Shop All (159K sessions/month) — highest volume, fastest to significance
  • Significance threshold: 95% minimum, 98% preferred before calling winner
  • JRB30 learning: "Make collection card clickable" had only 0.9% engagement — this was a tap-area fix, not a dedicated action. V2 quick-add test is a different hypothesis (explicit ATC button) and should not be conflated with JRB30.
  • JRB22 (Collection Page Filters) status: Documented in registry but outcome not recorded — check with Convertibles before re-running filter test to avoid duplicating inconclusive work.
