MB Quiz Relaunch — March 3, 2026

A new MB quiz launched on March 3. Week 1 saw a traffic spike (14K sessions on launch day, 3x normal) that brought low-intent curiosity traffic and dragged rev/session down to $4.51. By Week 2, the new quiz recovered to $6.69/session — just 1.5% below the old quiz's $6.79. March 14 hit $8.15/session, the highest single day in the entire dataset. The new quiz needs more clean data (post-Heatmap fix) to make a definitive comparison.

Why Face Pencil Wins

The Face Pencil quiz converts at $9.02/session — 2.5x the MB quiz. Three things differentiate it:

1. Focused product scope. One product, one shade to pick. No coverage preference, no skin type, no routine questions. The quiz is just: pick your shade family, pick your model, done.

2. Highest purchase intent. People searching for a Face Pencil shade are further down the funnel — they already know the product and just need the right color.

3. Fewest steps. The Octane shade-finder format is quick: model grid → specific shade → results. No interstitials, no undertone questions, no review-your-answers screens.

The Pattern Is Clear

The quizzes that convert best ask the fewest questions. The Octane quizzes skip skin type, skin concerns, routine, coverage, undertone, name, and email — and convert at 2.5x the rate. Every additional step is a leak in the funnel. The new prototype adds useful context (multi-product matching) but needs aggressive step reduction to avoid repeating the MB quiz's problems.

Prototype Review: Strengths & Risks

The consolidated complexion quiz prototype at jrb-complexion-quiz.vercel.app introduces smart multi-product matching. But it also inherits several known conversion killers from the MB quiz.

What's Strong

  • Multi-product results page: matching across 5 products in one quiz is a powerful AOV driver. "Your Full Complexion Routine" cross-sell is excellent
  • "Let My Answers Decide" option: reduces decision fatigue for product selection. Smart default
  • Shade Match Guarantee messaging: 30-day returns, 45-day exchanges, and "Not your shade?" language directly addresses the #1 customer friction (shade uncertainty)
  • Bobbi Brown loading quote: builds trust during the processing delay. Social proof from the founder
  • Phase navigation (About You / Shade / Matches): 3 clear phases is better framing than "01/14"
  • Dark circle color question: smart way to match Neutralizer shade without requiring undertone knowledge
  • FAQ section on results: pre-empts post-quiz objections (product differences, sensitive skin, layering)

What Needs Work

  • "01 / 14" step counter still visible: same commitment anxiety issue that causes 56% intro drop on MB quiz. The phase labels are there — use them instead
  • Undertone question survives: the undertone fork (know it / help me / visual match) is better than MB's version, but the Octane quizzes skip it entirely and convert 2x better
  • Two "review your answers" screens: one mid-quiz (step 10) and one pre-results (step 18). Neither adds value — they just add steps and exit points
  • Product education interstitial (step 11): good info but adds a non-question step that inflates count. Make it inline on results
  • Name + Email capture before results: gating results behind data capture kills completion. The Octane quizzes capture zero data pre-results and convert better
  • 14 steps still too many: data says 5-step quizzes convert 2.5x better than 18-step. 14 is closer to 18 than to 5
  • No model-based shade matching: the Octane quizzes use real model photos for shade selection. The prototype uses shade family buttons (text labels). Visual matching is proven easier on mobile

Data-Driven Recommendations

Prioritized changes to the consolidated quiz prototype, each backed by performance data from the existing quizzes and customer intelligence research.

1

Kill the "01/14" Step Counter Critical

Replace with the phase navigation that's already in the prototype (About You / Shade Discovery / Your Matches). Three phases feel manageable. Fourteen steps feel like a tax form. The MB quiz's "01/18" counter correlates with its 56% intro drop-off.

Evidence: MB quiz loses 75K users at intro with "01/18" visible. Octane quizzes show no step count and convert 1.4–2.5x better.
2

Remove or Make Undertone Optional Critical

64.8% of prospects can't identify their undertone. The Octane quizzes skip undertone entirely and convert at $5–9/session. If the shade-matching algorithm needs undertone, infer it from the shade family selection + skin concerns rather than asking directly.

Evidence: MB quiz undertone step has 24.1s avg time and 29% view drop. 64.8% undertone confusion rate (ListenLabs). Face Pencil quiz ($9.02) skips it entirely.
3

Cut to ~8 Steps by Collapsing Non-Questions Critical

Remove both "review your answers" screens. Move product education to the results page. Make the testimonial inline (not a full screen). This cuts 4 steps immediately: 14 → 10. Then evaluate whether skin concerns and coverage preference can be inferred from routine + product selection to cut 2 more.

Evidence: Octane quizzes convert at $6.84 avg with ~5 steps. Every step beyond 8 correlates with declining rev/session across all JRB quiz data.
4

Add Visual Model-Based Shade Matching High Impact

All three Octane quizzes use real model photos for shade selection, not text labels (VERY PALE, PALE, FAIR...). The prototype uses text buttons for shade family. Visual matching is faster, more accurate, and works better on mobile where buttons are small.

Evidence: All 3 model-based quizzes outperform the text-based MB quiz. The Foundation Stick visual matching step in the prototype already uses models — extend this to the shade family step too.
5

Move Email/Name Capture to Post-Results High Impact

The prototype asks for name and email before showing results. This gates the payoff behind data capture. The Octane quizzes capture zero data before results and convert at 1.4–2.5x. Move the capture to after results: "Email me my shade matches" as an optional save step.

Evidence: None of the top 3 converting quizzes require data entry before results. Post-results email capture typically has higher quality leads anyway (they've seen the recommendation and want to save it).
6

Keep the Multi-Product Results Page Validated

The "Your Full Complexion Routine" cross-sell, shade match guarantee, and FAQ section are strong prototype additions. This is the biggest differentiator from the single-product Octane quizzes. The opportunity is to match Octane-level efficiency getting TO the results page, then beat Octane on AOV with the multi-product recommendation.

Evidence: Quiz landing hub ($5.79/session) shows users respond well to multi-product quiz framing. Face Pencil proves single-product quizzes convert but have a lower AOV ceiling.
7

Test a "Quick Match" vs "Full Profile" Fork Test Idea

Offer two paths from the intro: "Quick Match" (shade family → model match → results, 3 steps) for users who know what they want, and "Full Profile" (skin type, concerns, coverage, shade → results) for users who want guidance. Let the user choose their commitment level.

Evidence: The existing Octane quizzes prove the "quick match" model works ($5–9/session). The prototype's skin profiling adds value for multi-product matching but may not be needed for users who just want one product's shade.
