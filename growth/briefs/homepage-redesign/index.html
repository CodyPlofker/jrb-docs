Implication

The homepage serves multiple modes: restockers who need fast nav, browsers who want discovery, researchers who want depth, and a mixed majority who shift between modes. Architecture must support all three patterns -- fast paths to product AND rich content for exploration. Mobile-first is non-negotiable.

1.9

7 Key Tensions

Where data sources conflict or create tradeoffs. Every homepage decision requires navigating these tensions.

1
New Customer vs Returning Customer Homepage
Returning customers want a dashboard (what's new, what's on sale, take me to what I want). Prospects need a storefront (who are you, why should I trust you, help me figure out what to buy).
Resolution: Above-fold serves both (new arrivals + trust bar). Below-fold can layer in prospect-specific content (Bobbi, shop by concern, social proof) that returners can scroll past. The core thesis of this redesign.
2
Bobbi Content -- High Demand but Not Top Priority
45.1% say Bobbi content is MISSING. But only 33% rank it in their top 3 priorities. Bobbi importance is 4.20/5. For prospects who don't know Bobbi, the founder story needs more prominence.
Resolution: Bobbi is a "vitamin, not a painkiller." Integrate Bobbi INTO product sections (Bobbi's Picks, short video clips, "Bobbi recommends" tags) rather than as a standalone editorial section. For prospects: "Founded by Bobbi Brown" as a trust bar element.
3
Age Representation vs Brand Aspirationality
65+ cohort is the least satisfied (6.7% say it's fine). Multiple 45+ prospects felt the homepage skewed Gen Z. But younger prospects want energy, and Tracksuit shows the brand aspires to reach 35+.
Resolution: Cast across ages (35-65), don't skew old OR young. Bobbi as the generational bridge. Models with real skin, not just diverse casting with perfect skin. "Aspirational but inclusive."
4
Quiz / Shade Finder -- Can't Find It, Don't Trust It
17.6% of customers say the quiz should be easier to find. But 0% use it first. And 4/23 prospects who tried it felt it was a marketing funnel (email gate before results).
Resolution: Don't make the quiz a homepage hero. Surface "Send a Selfie" (unique, trusted by both groups) and put shade tools contextually near product displays. Consider ILIA's model: "Find My Shade" as a nav link, not a homepage section.
5
Product Organization -- Categories vs Concern vs Curated Looks
37.4% go straight to nav (want categories). 39.6% want skin-type organization. Prospects want curated "looks" and guided entry points.
Resolution: Offer multiple pathways: category nav for directed shoppers, "Shop by Concern" section for researchers, curated routine/look sections for browsers. Not one-size-fits-all.
6
Promotions -- High Priority, Brand-Cheapening Risk
81.3% rank promos in top 3 for homepage. But this is a loyal-customer sample who actively seeks deals. Over-indexing on promos can cheapen brand perception for prospects. Charlotte Tilbury leads with product story.
Resolution: Promo/announcement bar is sufficient. Don't make promotions the homepage hero. Let new arrivals and product content lead; promos serve as secondary pull.
7
Editorial Content -- Customers Reject It, Prospects Need It
Editorial/brand story ranked dead last (avg 7.44/8) by customers. But 5/23 prospects couldn't figure out who JRB is or what differentiates them.
Resolution: Don't create a "brand story" section. Weave differentiation into every element: "76K+ reviews" as a trust signal, "Founded by Bobbi Brown" as a credential, "Clean, strategic formulations" as a product descriptor. Let the brand story emerge from the product experience.

3.3

The 4 Variations

Control Current Homepage

The existing JRB homepage as-is. No changes. This is the baseline against which all variations are measured.

V1 + New Arrivals Section

Add a prominent "New at Jones Road" section below the hero.

  • 4-product grid with product images, star ratings, prices, and review counts
  • One featured review quote per product
  • Must be genuinely updated as new products launch
  • This is the single highest-demand change -- 94.5% top-3 priority, 49.5% say it's missing
What we learn: Does surfacing new products alone drive more engagement and revenue?
V2 V1 + Trust Bar + Social Proof

Everything in V1, plus:

  • Trust bar near top: "76,000+ Reviews | Free Shipping $55+ | Free Returns | Clean Formula"
  • Social proof section lower: Stats (76K reviews, 4.3 avg rating, 80% recommend) + 3 mini customer review quotes
  • Trust bar addresses the #1 prospect need: 16/23 cite reviews as primary trust signal, 10/23 search for return policy
What we learn: Do trust signals help prospects convert? Does explicit social proof add incremental value over V1?
V3 V2 + Bobbi Section

Everything in V2, plus:

  • Two-column Bobbi section: Square portrait + founder quote about why she started Jones Road
  • "Founded by Bobbi Brown" integrated into the trust bar
  • Tests whether Bobbi's presence on the homepage adds conversion lift beyond trust signals
  • 76.9% of existing customers rate Bobbi 4 or 5 out of 5; but does her presence help convert NEW customers?
What we learn: Does Bobbi's presence on the homepage add incremental conversion lift? Is the founder story a conversion lever or just a brand-health element?
V4 V3 + Full Redesign

Everything in V3, plus:

  • "Shop by Concern" interactive section: "I want to... glow / simplify my routine / go clean / hydrate"
  • Redesigned bestsellers with proof signals matching the new arrivals pattern (ratings, reviews, prices)
  • GWP banner section for current promotional offer
  • Routine builder section: "Your 5-Minute Morning" with 4 products + step numbers
  • This is the full proposed architecture from Tab 2
What we learn: Does the full experience compound the gains, or do the core changes (V1-V3) capture most of the value?

3.4

Outcome Interpretation

What each possible result tells us and what we do next.

OutcomeInterpretationNext Step
V1 wins New Arrivals alone drives engagement; trust/Bobbi/full redesign are neutral Ship V1, iterate on other sections separately in follow-up tests
V2 > V1 Trust signals help acquisition; incremental value from proof points Ship V2, test Bobbi section as a separate A/B
V3 > V2 Bobbi adds measurable incremental value on the homepage Ship V3, consider expanding to full redesign
V4 wins biggest Full experience compounds -- each layer adds value Ship V4 as the new homepage
V1 = V2 = V3 = V4 Only New Arrivals matters; additional changes are neutral noise Ship V1, reallocate design effort to PDP optimization
Control wins Current homepage is actually better; our assumptions were wrong Re-examine assumptions; test individual sections with different designs

The Key Question This Test Answers

Is homepage performance driven by a single high-impact change (New Arrivals) or by a compounding full experience? The progressive stack design means we'll know exactly where the value plateaus -- if V4 doesn't beat V2, we know the extra sections aren't worth the build effort.

3.6

Risk Mitigation

1
Progressive stacking isolates risk. If ANY variation hurts performance, we can identify exactly which change caused it. No need to untangle multiple simultaneous changes.
2
V1 is the lowest-risk change. It only adds a section -- nothing from the control is removed. Even if it's neutral, there's minimal downside.
3
Each variation only ADDS to the previous. Nothing from the control is removed at any stage. The worst case for any variation is that the new section is neutral, not harmful.
4
20% traffic caps exposure. If V4 (the most aggressive change) hurts performance, only 20% of visitors see it. Risk is bounded.
5
Segment analysis catches audience-specific effects. If trust signals help prospects but annoy returners, the new-vs-returning segment analysis will reveal it before we ship.
6
Novelty monitoring. We'll check performance at 1 week vs 2+ weeks to separate genuine improvement from novelty effects (new sections getting clicks because they're new, not because they're better).

Bottom Line

This test design is inherently conservative. We're adding, not removing. We're isolating, not bundling. And we're grounding every change in quantitative evidence from 91 survey responses and qualitative evidence from 23 prospect recordings. The research says what customers want -- this test measures whether giving it to them actually drives revenue.
